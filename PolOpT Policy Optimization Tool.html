Policy Optimization Tool (PolOpT)

An FAO decision-support tool that helps governments find the policy and spending mix that delivers the most across competing goals — economic, social and environmental — under a fixed budget. For drylands, it is the analytical bridge between what the Dryland Sustainable Landscapes Impact Programme (DSL-IP) proves on the ground and the national policies that can scale it.

Policy Optimization Tool · At a glanceFrom field evidence to national policy

The DSL-IP proves what works inside a landscape — community seed banks, green value chains, farmer field schools. The Policy Optimization Tool (PolOpT), developed by FAO's Monitoring and Analysing Food and Agricultural Policies (MAFAP) programme, answers the next question: which policies and public expenditures should a government choose so that those proven practices scale — without trading away the environment for growth, or growth for the environment. It does this by simulating and optimizing policy scenarios against multiple objectives at once.

In one sentence

PolOpT takes a government's budget and policy options and computes the combination that best advances economic, social and environmental objectives together — giving the DSL-IP a way to show that its drylands practices are not just good projects, but good policy.

01  A shared language

DSL-IP speaks in landscapes and practices; ministries of finance speak in budgets and returns. PolOpT translates one into the other.

02  Trade-offs made visible

It quantifies what is gained and lost when a government shifts spending — for example from fertilizer subsidies toward green value chains.

03  Evidence that scales

Practices validated in a few landscapes become scenarios a country can budget for nationally, grounded in practical, scalable solutions.

The toolWhat PolOpT actually does

PolOpT is a policy simulation and optimization tool. Rather than asking a government to guess which interventions to fund, it searches across the available policy levers — subsidies, public investments, extension services, value-chain support — and identifies the mix that maximises a chosen objective, or balances several objectives, given the resources available.

Figure 1 · How PolOpT works   Policy levers, a budget ceiling and a costed evidence base feed an optimization engine that weighs economic, social and environmental objectives — and returns the policy mix that performs best, with its outcomes made explicit. Schematic.
The environmental objective (EO)

What makes PolOpT relevant to drylands is that the environmental objective is a first-class goal, not an afterthought. A government can ask PolOpT to prioritise environmental outcomes — biodiversity, resilience, reduced land degradation — and see exactly what policy mix delivers them, and at what cost to the other objectives. That is the same balance the DSL-IP's Sustainable Landscape Production Framework strikes in the field.

Why it matters hereHow PolOpT supports drylands

The DSL-IP, through its Sustainable Landscape Production Framework (SLPF), is closely aligned with the goals and methods of PolOpT — particularly in how both integrate community-based and environmentally sustainable agricultural practices. The DSL-IP generates the evidence; PolOpT turns that evidence into policy options a finance ministry can act on.

01

DSL-IP demonstrates seed banks, FFS and green value chains, and measures their costs and outcomes.

02

The synergy, in short

DSL-IP optimises land use through on-the-ground interventions — agroforestry, soil conservation, crop diversification. PolOpT optimises land use through policy simulation. Together they ensure that policy recommendations are grounded in practical, scalable solutions, and that practical solutions reach the scale only policy can give them.

The core alignmentSLPF ↔ PolOpT, point by point

Five strands of the DSL-IP's Sustainable Landscape Production Framework map directly onto what PolOpT can model. In each, the DSL-IP supplies a proven practice and PolOpT supplies the policy lens that lets a country weigh it against the alternatives.

A concrete signalThe extension finding from Nigeria

One of the clearest illustrations of PolOpT's value for the DSL-IP comes from its Nigeria application, where the analysis pointed to a consistent relationship: increased investment in extension services correlates with better environmental and economic outcomes. That matters because Farmer Field Schools — the DSL-IP's flagship extension approach — are exactly the kind of service such a finding would justify scaling.

What it suggests

Where a government treats extension as a cost to be trimmed, PolOpT's analysis reframes it as an investment with a double return — productivity and the environment moving in the same direction rather than against each other.

Why it travels to drylands

The DSL-IP already runs FFS across its child projects. A PolOpT scenario that scales FFS-based extension turns that operational choice into a budgeted national policy, with the environmental gains made explicit.

Policy coherenceMeeting global commitments, credibly

Drylands policy does not sit in isolation — it answers to the Rio Conventions, the Global Forest Goals and United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification (UNCCD) COP decisions. The DSL-IP contributes to Land Degradation Neutrality (LDN) and supports those commitments; PolOpT gives countries the analytical backbone to show, in budget terms, how a policy shift moves them toward the targets they have signed.

Land Degradation Neutrality

PolOpT can demonstrate how reallocating expenditure toward sustainable land and forest management advances LDN targets rather than eroding them.

UNCCD & the Rio Conventions

Scenario outputs translate convention commitments into concrete, costed policy choices a ministry can defend and report against.

Forest & landscape coherence

By reducing pressure on Miombo and Mopane forests through SLM and diversified, mixed land-use systems, drylands policy can align agricultural investment with forest goals.

From commitment to coherence

International goals are often expressed as intentions; budgets are where they are kept or broken. By putting the environmental objective on the same footing as the economic one, PolOpT helps a government build policy coherence — the consistency across ministries and programmes that the Global Forest Goals and the Rio Conventions call for.

Where DSL-IP could startPractical entry points

The alignment is not only conceptual — it suggests concrete places to begin. Community Seed Banks and trees-on-farm, in particular, make strong candidates for a first PolOpT scenario, and countries could be clustered to work on shared cases.

  • Community Seed Banks as a first scenario. A well-bounded, evidence-rich practice with a clear environmental objective — an ideal candidate for an initial PolOpT scenario and a demonstration of the SLPF-to-policy bridge.
  • Cluster countries around shared cases. Rather than one scenario per country, child projects facing similar policy questions could be grouped — letting a single modelling effort inform several national dialogues.
  • Green value chains versus conventional subsidies. A high-visibility trade-off many drylands governments already face, where PolOpT can make the case for reallocation concrete.
  • FFS-based extension at scale. Building directly on the Nigeria signal, modelling what scaled extension would cost and return across a drylands portfolio.
Exploratory framing

These entry points are scoping ideas under discussion, not committed workstreams. Any PolOpT application would be developed jointly with the relevant Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) divisions, the MAFAP programme and national partners, and validated before use in policy dialogue.

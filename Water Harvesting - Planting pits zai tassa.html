Water Harvesting · In-situ conservation

Planting pits (zaï / tassa)

Hand-dug pits that concentrate rain, soil and manure for each plant

What it is

Planting pits are hand-dug pits dug across crusted, degraded land that concentrate the little rain that falls – plus wind-blown soil and added manure – into each pit, restoring crop and tree growth on ground that had gone bare and dead. They are known as zaï in Burkina Faso and tassa in Niger.

The pit does several jobs at once: it captures runoff and the seeds it carries, it holds the manure that draws termites which open up the hard soil, and it gives each plant a pocket of moisture and fertility. In the WOCAT classification it belongs to the in-situ moisture conservation group, and it works best combined with contour stone lines.

How it works

  • Dig pits ~20–30 cm across in a grid across the field, in the dry season ahead of the rains
  • Add manure or compost to each pit – this draws termites, whose tunnelling opens the crusted soil and improves infiltration
  • Plant cereals or tree seedlings in the pit, where the concentrated water and nutrients give them a start
  • Let runoff and wind-blown seeds collect in the pits, so they trap both water and planting material
  • Combine with contour stone lines, which slow and spread the sheet flow feeding the pits

Pits plus stone lines

Zaï pits work best combined with contour stone lines: the lines slow and spread the sheet flow, the pits capture and hold it around each plant – together they have reclaimed large areas of crusted Sahelian land.

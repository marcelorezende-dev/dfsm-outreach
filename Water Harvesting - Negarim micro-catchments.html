Water Harvesting · Micro-catchment

Negarim micro-catchments

A closed grid of diamond basins, each feeding one tree

What it is

Negarim micro-catchments are a closed grid of small, diamond-shaped earth basins, each one draining to an infiltration pit at its lowest corner where a tree or shrub is planted. The bare diamond is the catchment; the pit is the cultivated point, so every unit harvests the runoff from its own little surface and delivers it to a single plant. It is the most widely used micro-catchment for tree establishment in the drylands (Critchley & Siegert, 1991; Oweis, 2001).

Because the diamonds are laid out as a continuous, interlocking grid, the technique treats a whole field at once rather than scattered spots. In the WOCAT classification it belongs to the micro-catchment water-harvesting group, where a short bare catchment feeds an immediately adjacent planted area.

How it works

  • Peg out a diamond grid on the contour across the field
  • Throw up small bunds on all four sides of each unit, closing every diamond
  • Orient each diamond so its longest diagonal runs down-slope to the planting pit at the lowest corner
  • Size the diamond to the slope, rainfall and tree water need, so the catchment delivers what the plant requires
  • Plant one tree per unit in the infiltration pit, where the harvested runoff collects

The genetic pool of options

Community Seed Banks secure crop diversity, agrobiodiversity and the social networks that let dryland communities keep producing — and restart after a shock — as climate change erodes the reliability of staple crops.

As stable food-crop production — especially maize — declines under climate change and soil degradation, drought-tolerant seed crops and diverse tree seedlings provide the genetic pool of options communities draw on to adapt. The CSB pillar of the SLPF builds that pool, and the local institutions to govern it.

Why diversity is resilience

Diverse beats uniformDiverse cropping systems are naturally more resilient; monocultures are highly vulnerable to shocks (Lin, 2011).
NutritionTraditional crops provide a broader range of essential nutrients, countering dietary deficiency (Kahane et al., 2013).
Recovery & sharingSeed banks maintain locally adapted varieties, let communities restart after disasters, and share knowledge and resources (Vernooy et al., 2017).

Where it sits in the framework

Assessment → sustainability

CSB runs the full cycle: agrobiodiversity survey and seed-system mapping; a seed-multiplication plan and species prioritization; seed-bank establishment and community training; diversity indices and adoption tracking; and seed-bank governance with exchange networks.

It powers the other pillars

CSB supplies the germplasm FFS demonstrates in the field and FFF turns into value chains — for example, higher-biomass pigeon-pea varieties in the Malawi IFES case. The pillars are strongest delivered together.

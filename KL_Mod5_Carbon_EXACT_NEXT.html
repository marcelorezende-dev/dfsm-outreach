Putting a carbon number on the change

EX-ACT and EX-ACT NEXT — FAO's carbon-balance tools that estimate the greenhouse-gas emissions and removals of land-use interventions, turning restoration into a measurable Global Environmental Benefit.

To justify investment and report to the GEF, the programme must estimate the climate benefit of what it does. The EX-Ante Carbon-balance Tool (EX-ACT) and its successor EX-ACT NEXT translate planned land-use changes into a carbon balance — net tonnes of CO₂-equivalent emitted or sequestered — grounded in IPCC methodology.

How it works

Define scenariosCompare a “without-project” baseline against the project's land-use interventions.
Enter the landLand-use change, cropland, agroforestry, forest, grassland, soil type, livestock and inputs.
Read the balanceThe tool applies IPCC factors (2006 / 2019) and returns net CO₂e emissions or removals.

Crucially, the soil-type classification comes from the field — the Simplified LADA ground-truthing — so the carbon estimate is anchored to observed conditions rather than assumed defaults. EX-ACT NEXT extends the tool with updated IPCC tables, country profiles and dedicated forest, cropland, grassland, wetland and coastal modules.

Why it matters

It closes the evidence chain

Land condition (Module 1) → field drivers (LADA) → soil & land-use inputs → carbon balance. The result populates the programme's Global Environmental Benefit indicators — the climate case for restoration, quantified.

It keeps claims honest

Ex-ante estimates are projections, not measured outcomes. Reported as indicative and revisited against monitoring, they guide design without overstating impact.

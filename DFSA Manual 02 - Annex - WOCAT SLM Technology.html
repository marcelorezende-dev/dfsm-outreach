2 · Description of the Technology

2.1Short description
Legal, planned harvest of native Caatinga for firewood and charcoal: the area is divided into annual cutting blocks worked on an approximately 15-year rotation; each block is coppice-cut near ground level, leaving seed trees and riparian strips, then left to regrow before its turn comes round again (MMA, 2011).
2.2Detailed description

The Northeast depends heavily on Caatinga firewood and charcoal; the difference between degradation and sustainability is how the wood is cut. Under a Sustainable Forest Management Plan (PMFS), the production area is inventoried and divided into roughly equal annual cutting blocks (talhões). One block is harvested per year on a cutting cycle of about 15 years, so each block has time to regrow before being cut again (MMA, 2011).

Harvest is by selective / coppice cutting near ground level in the dry season, exploiting the Caatinga's strong resprouting (rebrota); seed trees are retained and the riparian vegetation and legal reserve are never touched, with no burning of the cleared block. Cut wood is dried; for charcoal, improved kilns raise yield per tonne and cut smoke. The product is moved with the documentation from the management plan, typically through a community association or settlement, giving producers a legal, documented income (Araújo Filho, 2013; MMA, 2011).

Inventory-based sustainable yields observed in community plans range widely with site quality – on the order of 38–203 stacked-m³ (st) per hectare per harvest (MMA, 2008, 2011). Land users gain a lasting wood-energy income and keep the standing forest's other services; the main constraints are the cost and bureaucracy of the management plan, the labour of cutting, and protecting regenerating blocks from fire and over-grazing.

4 · Technical specifications, activities and costs

4.1Technical specifications
  • Cutting cycle ≈ 15 years; area divided into around 15 annual blocks (one cut/year)
  • Coppice cut near ground level to favour resprouting; seed trees retained
  • Riparian vegetation and legal reserve excluded from harvest; no fire
  • Indicative sustainable yield ≈ 38–203 st/ha per harvest (site-dependent; MMA, 2008, 2011)
  • Improved kilns for charcoal conversion

6.4 – 6.6Cost-benefit, adoption, adaptation
Cost–benefit
Short-term neutral/slightly positive; long-term positive (where legal market exists)
Adoption
Single cases / 1–10% – formalised PMFS still limited (WOCAT, 2019)
Adaptation
Yes – improved kilns; block size and cycle tuned to stock and demand
6.7 – 6.8Strengths and weaknesses

Strengths

  • Turns a driver of degradation into a renewable, legal income
  • Keeps the forest and its services; reduces fire
  • Coppice regrowth makes the Caatinga well-suited to rotation

Weaknesses → remedy

  • Plan cost and bureaucracy → simplified community PMFS; support
  • Illegal-wood competition → enforcement + market for legal product
  • Regenerating blocks vulnerable → fire and grazing control

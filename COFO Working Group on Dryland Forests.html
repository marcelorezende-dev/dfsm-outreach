Governance note · At a glanceWhat the Working Group is

The Committee on Forestry (COFO) Working Group on Dryland Forests and Agrosilvopastoral Systems is a subsidiary body of COFO – an inter-governmental, multi-stakeholder expert group that works to protect and restore drylands, enhance local livelihoods and boost resilience, recognising the need for the integrated development of forests, trees and livestock.

In one sentence

The Working Group is COFO's standing expert eye on the drylands – it reviews their status and trends, advises and reports to the Committee on Forestry, promotes the scaling-up of good restoration practice, and provides the inter-governmental governance that anchors FAO's dryland work, from the GEF-7 DSL-IP to DFSA itself.

Where it sitsIts relation to COFO

The Working Group does not stand alone. It is a subsidiary body of the FAO Committee on Forestry – established at COFO's 23rd session (2016) on the basis of Rule XXXI, paragraph 10 of the General Rules of the Organization, and operating in accordance with the General Rules and COFO's own Rules of Procedure. It is, in effect, the Committee's dedicated technical organ for drylands: it reviews the field and reports up, and the Committee guides its work and adopts its recommendations.

Why a dedicated Working Group?

At its inaugural session, FAO's Assistant Director-General for Forestry noted that the Working Group's expertise and knowledge would help COFO and its members make better-informed policies and take further action on the sustainable management of drylands and agrosilvopastoral systems. As a subsidiary body, it gives the Committee a standing, expert channel into a fragile ecosystem that covers 41 percent of the Earth's land surface – and a programmatic anchor for coherence and coordination across the dryland regions, including the GEF-7 DSL-IP and its global component. The Chair of COFO's 28th Session has described it as FAO's only subsidiary intersectoral technical body dedicated to drylands – a verdict confirmed by an independent review endorsed by COFO in 2024.

A short historyFrom a call for assessment to a global expert group

The Working Group grew out of COFO's recognition, in the mid-2010s, that drylands were under-assessed and under-invested. Its story tracks the maturing of FAO's whole dryland agenda – from the first global assessment, to the DSL-IP Impact Program, to the monitoring tools the Group itself called into being. Four sessions have been held since 2019 – Rome, Tanzania (virtual), Amman and Ulaanbaatar – with a fifth due in Zimbabwe.

Terms of ReferenceMandate – what it is asked to do

The Working Group's Terms of Reference, adopted by COFO, set four core functions. In short: review and report; build understanding; promote good practice; and account back to the Committee.

a  Review and report

Review and report to COFO on the status, trends, issues and developments in dryland forests and agrosilvopastoral systems – and make recommendations to the Committee.

b  Build understanding

As guided by the Committee, contribute to developing a comprehensive understanding of dryland forests and agrosilvopastoral systems – and of the people who depend on them.

c  Promote good practice

Promote the scaling-up of good practices for the protection, sustainable management and restoration of drylands, enhancing environmental and socio-economic resilience and sustainable livelihoods.

d  Account back

Report to the Committee on any other of its activities, in accordance with COFO's Rules of Procedure.

The activities behind the mandate

  • Assess and follow the status of dryland forests and agrosilvopastoral systems, and feed it into well-informed decision-making and policy.
  • Advise on capacity development needs for the sustainable management, restoration, assessment and monitoring of these systems.
  • Identify tools and approaches appropriate for drylands, and support their dissemination and adoption.
  • Share knowledge and lessons on sustainable management, restoration and monitoring – and promote their uptake into policy, investment and implementation.
  • Identify opportunities for collaboration and scaling-up within COFO, with other FAO technical committees and with partners.
  • Provide advice in support of the SDGs and the Rio Conventions – the UNCCD, the Convention on Biological Diversity, and the UNFCCC.
  • Undertake such other tasks as may be delegated to it by the Committee.

How it worksThe mechanics of an inter-governmental body

The Working Group runs on the quiet machinery set out in its Rules of Procedure – sessions, officers, a steering committee and a secretariat – designed so that an expert group spread across dozens of countries can still speak with one voice to COFO.

01
Sessions

Normally one session every two years, convened by the Director-General in consultation with the Chairperson. A majority of members forms a quorum.

02
Officers

At each session the Group elects a Chairperson and two Vice-Chairs, mindful of regional rotation and gender balance. They may not run for consecutive re-election.

03
Steering Committee

The Chair and two Vice-Chairs act as a Steering Committee during and between sessions – facilitating consultation on agendas, formats and preparations.

04
Secretariat

A Secretary appointed by the Director-General services the Group's work – based in FAO's Forestry Division, reachable at Drylands-WGCOFO@fao.org.

05
Report up

The Group's reports and recommendations flow to COFO and its members, all FAO Member Nations, observers and interested international organizations.

Membership

Open to all Members of COFO whose territories lie wholly or partly in drylands – or who are interested in contributing. Each Member designates a representative from appropriate sectors and disciplines.

Voting and decisions

Each Member has one vote. Decisions are ascertained by the Chairperson; on the request of one or more Members, the Group resorts to a vote under the General Rules.

Observers

Inter-governmental and non-governmental organizations attend as observers and were endorsed to provide notes and advice during discussions – IUCN, the GEF, the UNCCD, ICARDA, WWF, CGIAR and others.

Between sessions

The Steering Committee keeps the Group moving – consulting members, shaping the next agenda, and coordinating closely with the DSL-IP Global Steering Committee.

A two-way relationship with the Impact Program

The Group's Steering Committee holds a seat on the DSL-IP Global Steering Committee – alongside IUCN, the World Bank, WWF and WOCAT – so that guidance flows down into the eleven country (child) projects and lessons flow back up into COFO. Members are invited to dock their national dryland units with the child projects and to join the project Steering Committees in their own countries, keeping a continuous two-way flow of information between the field and the Committee.

Observers and partners around the table

Beyond its members, the Group draws on a wide circle of observer states (among them Botswana, Colombia, Iraq, Kazakhstan, Maldives, Namibia, Qatar, Somalia, Syria, Togo, Uganda and Viet Nam) and institutional observers – IUCN, the Global Environment Facility (GEF), the UNCCD, ICARDA, ICBA, CIFOR–ICRAF, ILRI, the International Land Coalition, SADC, the League of Arab States, WWF, CGIAR, Bioversity–CIAT and GIZ – who contribute notes, advice and technical validation during sessions.

Who runs itSecretariat, officers and steering committee

Day to day, the Group is carried by its Secretariat in FAO's Forestry Division; strategically, it is led by an elected Chair and two Vice-Chairs who together form the Steering Committee. Officers rotate at each session, with attention to regional balance and gender.

What it deliversWork plans and outputs

The Group works to a rolling work plan, structured around a handful of work areas and delivered “subject to the availability of extra-budgetary resources.” Three plans have run so far – two biennial, then, after an independent review, a single Multi-Year Work Plan with milestones reviewed at each session.

The origin storyThe Working Group and DFSA

For this platform, the Working Group is not just one more governance body to document – it is where DFSA came from. The line is direct and on the record.

DFSA was called for here

At its Inaugural Session in July 2019, the Working Group considered “the development of the platform for documenting and monitoring the different restoration initiatives implemented in dryland forests and agrosilvopastoral systems,” advised that it be tested and linked with country data, and recommended that FAO produce a user guide to build countries' capacity to use it. That platform was the Dryland Restoration Initiative Platform (DFSA) – today the Dryland Forest Support Accelerator you are reading now.

The relationship did not end with the recommendation. Across every work plan since, the Group has continued to steward DFSA's development: FAO tested it and integrated it into the Framework for Ecosystem Restoration Monitoring (FERM) for the UN Decade on Ecosystem Restoration; the integrated FERM–DFSA tools were piloted in 25 countries; and the Group's 2024–2027 Multi-Year Work Plan commits it to keep promoting DFSA within FERM, linked to Land Degradation Neutrality targets. Tellingly, FAO's own 2023–2025 session documents now style it the “Dryland Restoration Initiatives Platform (DFSA)” – the very plural the platform carries today.

2 · Description of the Technology

2.1Short description
Selective manipulation of native Caatinga woody vegetation – thinning, coppicing or enrichment – to multiply dry-season fodder while keeping around 40 percent tree cover, the seedbank and riparian strips intact, integrating livestock with the standing dry forest.
2.2Detailed description

The Technology raises the forage value of native Caatinga (seasonally dry tropical forest) without clearing it, by selectively managing the woody layer and the herbaceous layer for grazing by goats, sheep and cattle. Three complementary measures are applied according to the ecological potential of the site:

Thinning (raleamento): selective cutting of woody plants to reduce shade and release the herbaceous layer, keeping around 400 trees/ha (≈40% canopy) – forage species, deep-rooted and timber/landscape trees retained, no fire. Available forage rises to about six times that of untreated Caatinga.
Coppicing (rebaixamento): manual lowering of forage trees and shrubs (sabiá, jurema-preta, mororó, quebra-faca, pau-branco, feijão-bravo, camaratuba…) to bring browse within reach and extend green leaf into the dry season; forage rises to about four times native, around 60 percent of biomass coming from the woody layer.
Enrichment (enriquecimento): for already-degraded sites that have lost the herb layer – minimum-tillage reseeding of adapted native/hardy forage grasses with phosphate correction (≈100 kg/ha P₂O₅), keeping 150–200 trees/ha.

Whatever the measure, three rules guarantee sustainability: preserve up to 400 trees/ha (≈40% canopy); harvest at most 60 percent of the available forage; preserve riparian vegetation across the whole drainage network. Cutting is done in the dry season; regrowth (sprouts) is managed by cutting (roço) mid–wet-season and by browsing, with most stumps dying back within around five years. Animals are admitted only after the herb seeds have set.

Applied across the Brazilian semi-arid sertão, the Technology increases dry-season fodder and live-weight gain, sharply reduces drought-year production losses (around 22 percent vs around 80 percent on untreated Caatinga), conserves native biodiversity and soil, and yields firewood from the thinning that offsets establishment cost. Land users value the extra fodder and drought resilience while keeping wood, honey and fruit; the main constraints are labour for cutting/roço, site-dependent response, and licensing/credit barriers (Araújo Filho, 2013).

4 · Technical specifications, activities, inputs and costs

4.1Technical specifications
  • Retained canopy: around 400 trees/ha (≈40% cover) for thinning/coppicing; 150–200 trees/ha for enrichment
  • Forage off-take: ≤ 60% of available forage (≥40% left on the ground)
  • Riparian buffer retained along the entire drainage network
  • Species retained: sabiá (Mimosa caesalpiniifolia), jurema-preta (Mimosa tenuiflora), mororó (Bauhinia), quebra-faca, pau-branco, feijão-bravo, camaratuba
  • Enrichment inputs: ≈100 kg/ha P₂O₅; grasses (buffel, capim-corrente, gramão Aridus) sown in the rains
  • No fire at any stage

6.3Exposure and sensitivity to climate change
Exposed to: rising temperatures, declining/erratic rainfall, recurrent drought, occasional intense rainstorms. The Technology copes well with drought (its core purpose); herb-layer establishment is sensitive to the timing of the rains.

6.7Strengths / advantages

Land user

  • Much more dry-season fodder; far lower drought losses
  • Keeps wood, honey, fruit and shade from the standing forest
  • Low external inputs (thinning/coppicing); cost offset by firewood

Compiler / specialist

  • Conserves native biodiversity, soil and water – no exotic monoculture
  • Builds drought and climate resilience; reduces fire
  • Scalable across the sertão; aligns with agroecology and SLPF

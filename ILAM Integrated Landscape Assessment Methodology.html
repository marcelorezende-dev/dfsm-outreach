In one sentence

ILAM integrates wall-to-wall Earth observation screening, permanent sample-based land-use assessment, participatory group-based diagnostics, household resilience and behaviour-change surveys, and project-level carbon accounting — deployed as a national capacity-development process so that the monitoring infrastructure outlasts the project funding cycle.

IntroductionThe evidence challenge in landscape interventions

Land degradation cannot be captured through any single observation system. Remote-sensing products track land-cover change and productivity at broad scales, but at the landscape and farm level the causal mechanisms that decide whether degradation continues, stabilizes or reverses are fundamentally socio-economic and institutional — shaped by tenure, market access, labour, and the capacity of land users to adopt alternatives (Lambin et al., 20011).

This scale mismatch is quantifiably costly. Global land-cover products systematically underestimate dryland forest extent by margins large enough to exclude entire biomes from climate models and restoration plans (Bastin et al., 20172), while national-scale productivity indicators capture regional trends without resolving the management-driven dynamics that determine outcomes where intervention is actually feasible.

Four structural failures ILAM was built to address

Scale mismatch

Monitoring systems operate at scales different from where the processes of interest, and the decisions, actually occur.

Domain fragmentation

Biophysical, socio-economic and institutional evidence are commissioned as disconnected, single-purpose datasets.

Temporal discontinuity

Baselines are rarely designed for repeat measurement, so attribution at midterm or endline becomes impossible.

Reporting proliferation

The same landscape must feed UNCCD, UNFCCC, CBD and project frameworks — each with incompatible indicators and formats.

The compounding cost

The consequence is not merely administrative burden. Incompatible, single-purpose datasets cannot accumulate into a cumulative evidence base. National monitoring capacity, rather than deepening through successive assessment cycles, is perpetually rebuilt from zero — leaving countries structurally unable to demonstrate whether the aggregate of individual interventions is producing the landscape-level transformation that Land Degradation Neutrality requires.

The Rome Promise · COFO 2015

The FAO Committee on Forestry identified this convergence of structural failures as a barrier to effective dryland land management in its Rome Promise on Monitoring and Assessment of Drylands for Sustainable Management and Restoration (2015), calling explicitly to compile and integrate drylands monitoring tools toward comprehensive guidelines. ILAM is FAO's direct, operational response to that call.

The scientific consensus has long favoured frameworks that bridge biophysical and socio-economic evidence at the scale of actual management decisions (Sayer et al., 20133; Reed et al., 20164). The challenge has never been conceptual recognition — it has been practical operationalization: few programmes have possessed, at inception, the integrated data architecture that landscape governance requires. ILAM closes that gap through tools that are free, user-friendly and standards-compliant, while building the national monitoring capacity they need to function as a durable institutional asset.

Conceptual FoundationsAnchored in the science of Land Degradation Neutrality

ILAM was designed in close alignment with the UNCCD's Scientific Conceptual Framework for LDN (Orr et al., 20175). The framework prescribes a land-based approach with six core elements; ILAM operationalizes each one through its modules.

Stratify by land type

Remote-sensing Modules 1 & 2 provide the land-type stratification the framework requires.

Evaluate processes, causes & drivers

Module 3 focused-group discussions surface causes, drivers and barriers to sustainable management.

Avoid, reduce, reverse

The five modules generate evidence aligned to the LDN response hierarchy with geographic precision.

Assess resilience

Module 4 household surveys assess community resilience and the practical capacity to adopt improved practices.

Protect restored land

Repeat assessment infrastructure monitors whether gains are sustained against future degradation.

Neutrality planning

The evidence base feeds integrated land-use planning for LDN at sub-national scales.

Including — and going beyond — SDG 15.3.1

SDG Indicator 15.3.1 measures the proportion of degraded land through three biophysical sub-indicators. These are well suited to national-scale reporting, but at farm or community level they are not operationally useful for planning, even when calculated with the most advanced remote-sensing techniques (Sims et al., 20216). ILAM encompasses SDG 15.3.1 through its remote-sensing modules, then extends the evidence base to explain not only where degradation occurs but why it persists.

Land Degradation Neutrality (LDN)

A state whereby the amount and quality of land resources necessary to support ecosystem functions and services and enhance food security remain stable or increase within specified temporal and spatial scales. The DSL-IP supports eleven countries in operationalizing LDN at landscape level.

A socio-ecological systems lens

ILAM's combination of tools reflects socio-ecological systems theory. Following Ostrom's (2009) framework11, the sustainability of resource governance depends on the interactions between resource users, governance structures and biophysical conditions — land degradation cannot be addressed through biophysical interventions alone. ILAM therefore analyses three levels of causation:

  • Proximate drivers — land-use change, vegetation-cover loss, unsustainable management practices.
  • Indirect drivers — population pressure, poverty, insecure land tenure.
  • Differentiated land users — indigenous groups, land-forest-livestock-dependent households, gender-differentiated decision makers.

This multi-driver, multi-actor perspective is essential for designing interventions that are both technically sound and socially feasible.

Institutional ArchitectureA One-FAO methodology, born in a Community of Practice

The DSL-IP posed methodological challenges qualitatively different from conventional single-country projects. Designing and adaptively managing interventions across national contexts demanded evidence spanning remote sensing, land-management science, agricultural production, socio-economics, resilience analysis and institutional governance — a scope no single technical unit could cover alone.

The institutional origin · CoP1

A 2019 regional design workshop in Johannesburg brought together project teams, FAO country focal points, academia and governments from six countries to co-design and build capacity on ILAM. It became the programme's first major contribution to Community of Practice 1: Land Degradation Assessment and Decision Making — a pioneering multi-partner, cross-divisional, participatory structure under which specialists piloted, adapted and integrated the tools that compose ILAM.

Six FAO divisions, one toolkit

During design and throughout implementation, governments, project teams, partners and service providers collaborate with six FAO divisions through the community of practice. This six-division architecture reflects the breadth of expertise that landscape-scale land degradation demands.

Programme context & study area

The DSL-IP is the first GEF Impact Programme in which FAO serves as lead implementing agency. While it supports eleven countries in Africa and Central Asia, this methodology was applied across six child projects in the Miombo-Mopane woodlands of Southern Africa — one of the continent's most extensive woodland biomes, storing substantial carbon, supporting exceptional biodiversity, and providing livelihoods for an estimated 300 million people, yet facing accelerating pressure from agricultural expansion, overgrazing, unsustainable charcoal production and recurrent fire.

The Modular ApproachFive modules, one coherent architecture

ILAM comprises five complementary modules. Tool selection was driven by evidence from prior applications, alignment with the LDN conceptual framework, availability as free and user-friendly platforms, capacity-development potential, and compatibility with international reporting. Each module delivers independent value — but the methodology is most powerful when all five are systematically integrated.

Module 1 · Land Characteristics and Prioritization

Open Foris EarthMapGoogle Earth EngineDSL-IP GeoportalsWall-to-wall · qualitative

A qualitative, wall-to-wall landscape screening using freely available remote-sensing products accessed through FAO's EarthMap platform (Morales et al., 20237), built on Google Earth Engine and part of the Open Foris Initiative. A central function is the assessment of land productivity dynamics (LPD) using NDVI-based indicators — which in many instances account for over 90% of measured land degradation — revealing whether a landscape is improving or worsening, and locating degradation hotspots and recovery bright spots.

  • Integrates thematic layers: land cover, forest resources, fire regimes, livestock, cropland extent and aridity.
  • A living spatial registry: country teams upload georeferenced intervention polygons (reforestation, ANR, agroforestry, woodlots) as primary spatial means of verification.
  • Connects to two reporting frameworks — the FERM (UN Decade on Ecosystem Restoration) and the UNCCD PRAIS system for national LDN accounts.
  • Runs entirely in-browser with no installation, enabling real-time multi-stakeholder land-use planning with non-specialists.

Module 2 · Consistent Land Representation and Reporting

Open Foris Collect EarthAfrica GGWI frameworkSample-based · quantitativeIPCC 2006

Where Module 1 is qualitative, Module 2 delivers the quantitative, sample-based rigour required for formal reporting to the UNCCD, UNFCCC and other commitments. ILAM uses Open Foris Collect Earth for augmented visual interpretation of high-resolution imagery (Bey et al., 201610), producing accuracy comparable to or exceeding automated classification — particularly important in the Miombo-Mopane context, where wall-to-wall mapping yielded unacceptable uncertainty in dryland forest cover.

Sampling is a methodological choice, not a constraint

Wall-to-wall products systematically misclassify dryland forest (Bastin et al., 2017). Sampling inherently generates confidence intervals, and a permanent plot grid means the reporting period can be defined post-assessment without rebuilding the dataset — breaking the cycle of commissioning new maps each reporting round.

  • Built on FAO's SIGRID systematic grid (1×1 km, sub-nested), densified to 2 km or 1 km for landscape and project scales — always on the same axis for cross-scale compatibility.
  • Each plot is 70×70 m with 49 control points; the full land-use history is reconstructed from 2000 onward following IPCC six land-use categories, with management factors enabling Tier-2 soil-carbon estimates.
  • A do-it-once design: a single grid serves SDG 15.3.1 (UNCCD), the AFOLU GHG inventory (UNFCCC), REDD+ FRELs, SDG 15.1 (FAO FRA) and the national forest inventory.
  • Interpretation is conducted by multidisciplinary, cross-ministerial LDN working groups, building national capacity and ownership.
Box · The Africa Open DEAL & GGWI continental baseline +

The approach underpinning Module 2 was validated at continental scale through the Africa Open Data for Environment, Agriculture and Land initiative, coordinated by FAO and the African Union Commission with the Great Green Wall agency and SADC. Between 2018 and 2020, a continental assessment across 30 African countries assembled more than 300,000 sampling points through 17 trainings and mapathons run by ~350 trained interpreters.

It made Africa the first continent to produce a complete, harmonized, statistically robust land-use dataset — revealing 7 billion previously unrecorded trees outside forests and substantially more dryland forest and arable area than any prior assessment. Its cross-border statistical consistency is why it is the reference framework adopted by the DSL-IP for all Collect Earth assessments.

Example · Botswana's National Land Degradation Monitoring System +

Before the DSL-IP, Botswana's land-degradation statistics were “complex and difficult to come by.” Global default data overestimated degraded land at 51.4% of territory, versus 32.6% when nationally validated metrics were applied (Akinyemi et al., 20218) — a near-19-point gap that shows the cost of relying on global products for national decisions.

From September 2020 the Department of Forestry and Range Resources, with FAO, established a permanent, plot-based, visually interpreted national monitoring system — densifying the 4 km grid to 1 km in the Chobe and Makgadikgadi/Tutume landscapes. It is now capable of generating the UNCCD PRAIS-compliant SDG 15.3.1 data Botswana previously lacked.

Module 3 · Focused-Group Discussions and Multi-Stakeholder Assessments

Simplified LADA-WOCATFFPO mappingCNAValue chainsPolicy & institutional

Module 3 is the participatory core of ILAM — all landscape-level, group-based assessments conducted through facilitated focused-group discussions and multi-stakeholder workshops, deploying five nested components that share a common participatory architecture.

FFPO assessment & targeting

Forest & Farm Producer Organizations profiled across 48 parameters in seven dimensions, with SWOT analysis. 226+ FFPOs across 20 landscapes — from 9-member beekeeping groups to cooperatives over 41,000 members.

Value-chain analysis

Selection against eight criteria distinguishes conservation-aligned chains (honey rewards intact woodland) from degradation-driving ones (charcoal, tobacco).

Capacity Needs Assessment

Nine thematic modules feeding each country's ICDIP, in a structured challenge–capacity–need format, with iterative quality-assurance callbacks.

Simplified LADA & ground-truthing

Field assessment at the same georeferenced Collect Earth plots — a direct validation pathway between satellite signal and field observation, with a five-photo protocol.

Stakeholder & policy-institutional

Three nested levels — enabling environment, organizational, individual — using a ~35-question CNAn and the UNCCD LDN TPP Checklist (40 items).

Example · Tanzania FFPO assessment — Ugalla & Mlele landscapes +

Conducted in two phases across four districts, assessing 51 FFPOs. The MSG value-chain ranking prioritized honey — the only high-value activity that economically rewards intact woodland, since bee forage depends on standing forest. The highest-priority honey facility had a 30-tonne/month capacity but operated at just 1% due to working-capital shortfalls. The assessment surfaced a key trade-off: whether to include tobacco-linked cooperatives with strong tree-nursery capacity, given that tobacco curing drives charcoal demand — resolved by decoupling support from tobacco while strengthening their restoration functions.

Example · Angola value-chain assessment — Cuando & Cunene +

Two provinces with contrasting profiles — crop-oriented Cuando, livestock-dominated Cunene — yet extremely low production: fertilizer use of 4 kg/capita/yr against 28 in Zambia and 110 in Brazil, with the 2019 harvest largely destroyed by drought affecting 2.3M people. Honey ranked highest despite a near-absent sector: one trained beekeeper can produce 200–400 kg/yr at USD 2/kg in Luanda. The binding constraint was the near-total absence of aggregation infrastructure — 80% of production traded informally, 40%+ lost to spoilage — establishing collective aggregation as the foundational investment priority.

Example · Malawi CNA & callback exercise — Balaka District +

A CNA across five FFPOs structured around the Integrated Food and Energy System core theme (pigeon-pea/maize intercropping and fuel-efficient stoves), followed by a quality-assurance callback covering six of the nine modules. It documented constraints and existing capacities invisible to the household survey alone — and demonstrated that the nine-module structured format makes data gaps diagnosable and correctable within the same assessment cycle, establishing iterative QA as a standard Module 3 protocol.

Example · Zimbabwe — institutionalizing the FFPO approach +

Across the Save and Runde sub-basins the FFPO targeting framework produced outcomes beyond the project boundary. In Chivi, the Rural District Council allocated land to four FFPOs for Marula oil production — conferring tenure security that incentivizes long-term restoration investment. In 2025, Chivi formally incorporated LDN activities into its district development plan and financed them through national devolution funds, with Buhera and Masvingo following — the transition from donor pilot to government-budgeted programme that the approach is designed to enable.

Module 4 · Household Survey (SHARP+ and COM-B)

SHARP+KoboCollectCOM-B frameworkGeoreferenced · household

SHARP+ is a tablet-based household resilience assessment — FAO's SHARP tool (2016) adapted by the DSL-IP with land-degradation, forest-management and ecoregion-specific calibrations. Administered via KoboCollect, it assesses resilience across agronomic, environmental, social and economic domains. Every record is georeferenced, enabling direct overlay with Module 1 and 2 spatial layers, and is applied at baseline and endline to measure whether capacity development has improved household resilience and practices.

COM-B · diagnosing why practices aren't adopted

SHARP+ integrates a behaviour-change assessment through the Capability, Opportunity, Motivation – Behaviour framework (Michie et al., 20119). It addresses a core failure of conventional extension — prescribing technical solutions without diagnosing why they aren't adopted.

Capability barriers

Knowledge and skill deficits — e.g. no awareness that agroforestry intercropping maintains soil fertility.

Opportunity barriers

Structural constraints — no nurseries for planting material, insecure tenure, weak market access, no aggregation infrastructure.

Motivation barriers

The cost-benefit calculus — where returns from sustainable practice are uncertain or delayed versus degradation-driving alternatives.

By diagnosing which barriers are operative at household level, ILAM enables interventions that are demand-responsive rather than supply-driven. Data is disaggregated by gender and age throughout, feeding directly into the ICDIP — mapping COM-B diagnostics onto Farmer Field School topics, Community Seed Bank interventions and Forest & Farm Facility business support. As the third tier in ILAM's evidence architecture, SHARP+ triangulates with the remote sensing of Modules 1–2 and the participatory assessments of Module 3.

Example · Tanzania SHARP+ baseline — Ugalla & Mlele +

Across four districts the survey confirmed the three principal degradation drivers: 46% of households in crop production, 43% in charcoal or firewood, 32% in livestock. Only 11% of village respondents were aware of any relevant government policy — corroborating the Module 3 enabling-environment deficit. COM-B analysis identified opportunity as the dominant barrier to improved beekeeping (modern hives ~USD 10 vs USD 0.50 for bark hives, plus no aggregation infrastructure), pointing ICDIP investment toward subsidised hives and processing centres rather than awareness-raising alone.

Module 5 · Carbon Balance Estimation (EX-ACT and NEXT)

FAO EX-ACTNEXTIPCC-based · AFOLUGEF Core Indicator 6

Module 5 estimates project-level greenhouse-gas emissions and removals using FAO's EX-ACT (Ex-Ante Carbon-balance Tool) or NEXT, providing a consistent IPCC-based methodology for AFOLU investments. It compares a without-project against a with-project scenario over a defined accounting period, drawing land-management detail from Module 3 and household production data from Module 4 — so estimate quality improves directly with the depth of upstream ILAM assessment.

Four functions within the DSL-IP

  • Direct support for GEF Core Indicator 6 (greenhouse-gas emissions mitigated).
  • Strengthening national capacity to estimate and monitor AFOLU emissions.
  • Supporting integration of mitigation objectives into Nationally Determined Contributions.
  • Providing transparent, country-specific estimates that improve on IPCC Tier-1 defaults.

The Angola project illustrates the output: over a 20-year accounting period, interventions across 25,212 ha produced a net carbon sink of −1,073,051 tCO₂-eq, against a baseline that would have been a net source. See the full balance in In Practice →

Integration & ApplicationsFrom integrated evidence to landscape action

The modules are described above as standalone instruments; ILAM's true power lies in their deliberate integration across scales and knowledge types. The architecture operates at three nested levels — and triangulation between them is one of ILAM's most important methodological contributions.

Level 1 · Satellite
Screen & quantify

Module 1 screens macro patterns wall-to-wall; Module 2 delivers locally validated, statistically robust assessments for formal reporting.

Level 2 · Landscape
Explain

Module 3's focused-group discussions link to the Collect Earth spatial framework, assessing degradation per land-use system.

Level 3 · Household
Validate & triangulate

SHARP+ ground-truths the remote sensing and refines the participatory findings at farm level.

Triangulation in action

Remote sensing may identify a zone of declining productivity; multi-stakeholder discussions reveal it is driven by overgrazing and arable expansion; FFPO assessments find whether organized producers exist to channel interventions; and SHARP+ establishes whether affected households have the capability, opportunity and motivation to adopt alternatives. Conversely, SHARP+ can reveal high food insecurity where remote sensing sees stable land condition — socio-economic degradation invisible to biophysical indicators alone.

ILAM across the project cycle

Project & programme design

Provides the evidence base on which interventions are targeted — replacing design based on assumptions or incomplete data.

Impact monitoring

Permanent sampling grids and baseline/endline SHARP+ surveys give guaranteed methodological continuity for attribution.

Land-use planning

Supplies the diagnostic baseline, scenarios and option appraisal that integrated land-use planning for LDN requires.

Applications in the Miombo-Mopane woodlands

The six-country application represents the first systematic deployment of an integrated landscape assessment methodology at programme scale across the ecoregion. Because each country deployed the same five-module architecture against shared data standards, the result is not a collection of national reports but a harmonized regional dataset — the 48-parameter FFPO database, regionally consistent SHARP+ resilience data, and cross-nationally compatible Collect Earth estimates — able to answer questions no single-country assessment could.

The Sustainable Landscape Production Framework (SLPF)

Within the DSL-IP, ILAM is the evidence-generating engine; the SLPF is the operational intervention mechanism it feeds. The SLPF is built on three integrated pillars working in concert:

Community Seed Banks (CSBs)

Promote crop diversification, agrobiodiversity conservation and enhanced social networks.

Forest & Farm Facility (FFF)

Business incubation and growth for FFPOs along green value chains.

Farmer Field Schools (FFSs)

Participatory, experiential learning platforms for technology transfer in SLM and SFM.

One Country Champion, One Core Theme

Rather than dispersing resources, each country selects a single core theme — identified through the ILAM evidence base — around which the SLPF pillars are integrated. The themes are nationally significant yet regionally transferable across the Miombo-Mopane ecoregion.

DiscussionPositioning, strengths and honest limits

ILAM addresses a critical gap in the LDN toolbox. While global monitoring of SDG 15.3.1 has advanced through platforms such as Trends.Earth and the UNCCD PRAIS system, there remains a deficit of integrated methodologies that connect landscape-level biophysical monitoring with the socio-economic realities of land users. ILAM fills this gap by making satellite data and farmer perspectives mutually validating sources of evidence, not parallel streams.

Relative to existing approaches

Trends.Earth

A powerful national-scale screening and reporting tool for the three SDG 15.3.1 sub-indicators. ILAM complements it with the landscape detail, ground-truthing and socio-economic dimensions that national remote sensing cannot capture — explaining why a district is declining and what will work.

PILA / FOLUR

FAO's Participatory Informed Landscape Approach shares ILAM's cross-divisional DNA. The distinction is the anchor: PILA is oriented to food-systems transformation, ILAM to Land Degradation Neutrality in drylands. The two are complementary and interoperable.

UNCCD default & NFMA

ILAM extends the default LDN reporting methodology by adding Tier-2 capability through Collect Earth, and enriches the forest-monitoring tradition with socio-economic and behaviour-change dimensions.

Four core strengths

01 Integration logic

Systematic triangulation across data sources and scales, connecting productivity trends to the motivations and barriers of farmers who must deliver LDN.

02 Evidence-based tools

Every component has a demonstrated track record and aligns with international standards.

03 Capacity development

Every assessment is simultaneously a training opportunity, building national technical infrastructure.

04 One-FAO foundation

Substantively multidisciplinary, not merely administratively cross-divisional.

Limitations and challenges

ILAM's comprehensive scope entails practical challenges that must be acknowledged.

Labour intensity & operator continuity +

Collect Earth visual interpretation is labour-intensive, requiring sustained operator engagement over weeks or months for national assessments. Sustaining trained operators and functioning LDN working groups beyond the project cycle depends on institutional commitment; staff turnover — a common reality — can erode the capacity base.

Module 1's qualitative ceiling +

The uncertainty of wall-to-wall products at fine scales cannot always be quantified; derived statistics are indicative rather than definitive. The 3-metre monthly imagery from the FAO-Norway partnership is currently limited to the tropics, constraining availability in the Central Asian DSL-IP countries.

Respondent fatigue & the breadth-simplicity trade-off +

SHARP+'s comprehensiveness can contribute to respondent fatigue, especially when combined with CNA discussions in the same communities. The do-it-once Collect Earth design trades breadth for simplicity: more survey cards mean richer data but longer training and higher QA requirements. The full five-module configuration is an ideal; resource constraints may lead countries to prioritize certain modules.

Geoportal sustainability & data sovereignty +

The DSL-IP geoportals advance centralized data integration, but their sustainability beyond project funding requires explicit planning for hosting, maintenance and governance. Free platforms address this partly, yet long-term national data-sovereignty considerations may call for transition to government-hosted infrastructure.

Conclusion

ILAM's central contribution is not technical novelty but the structured integration of existing best-in-class tools into a coherent evidence architecture — one that connects the global ambition of Land Degradation Neutrality to the farm-level realities of the land users who must ultimately deliver it. Its permanent assessment infrastructure — sampling grids, geoportal layers and household samples — outlasts individual project cycles, creating the institutional backbone for long-term national LDN monitoring.

A durable national asset

All ILAM tools are free, user-friendly and standards-compliant. Implemented as a national capacity-development process in close collaboration with government agencies, the methodology is designed so that the monitoring infrastructure it establishes outlasts the project funding cycle.

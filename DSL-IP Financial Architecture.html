Financial Architecture · At a GlanceThe model in one view

No single child project can afford the depth of technical expertise that integrated dryland management demands. The DSL-IP solves this not by spending more, but by pooling: each country's modest Lead Technical Officer (LTO) fee is combined with the resources of a Global Coordination Project (GCP), so that the same world-class specialists, tools and service providers serve every country at once. The result is a multiplier effect — small contributions, deep shared support.

In one sentence

The DSL-IP's financial architecture is a pooling mechanism: child-project fees pay for sparing, senior technical oversight (the focal points of seven FAO divisions), while the GCP pools global resources to fund the deep delivery (international consultants, regional service providers and a regional coordinator) — all converging at FAO's Subregional Office, where the Lead Technical Officer and the Regional Exchange Mechanism sit side by side.

The three design principles

01  Pool, don't fragment

Combine many small country fees with global resources so shared experts and providers serve all countries — economies of scale instead of eleven separate procurements.

02  Leverage One-FAO

Seven technical divisions contribute their specialty behind a single LTO — senior staff time used sparingly for quality, consultants for depth.

03  Build on existing infrastructure

Anchor the whole machine at the Subregional Office (SFS) that already exists — co-locating the LTO and the REM to minimize cost and maximize engagement.

The ChallengeWhy a financing architecture, not just budgets

Eleven child projects, each with a constrained budget, face the same paradox: integrated dryland management requires broad, specialist expertise — forestry, farmer field schools, seed systems, land tenure, land degradation neutrality, agribusiness, private-sector finance — yet no single national budget can afford to retain all of it, at depth, for five years. Procured separately and project-by-project, technical assistance becomes thin, duplicative and expensive.

Fragmented financing fails twice

If each project hired its own consultants for each discipline, it would pay full transaction costs for shallow coverage — and the lessons learned in one country would never reach the next. The result is high cost, low depth, and no knowledge flow. The same dollar, pooled, buys far more.

What the architecture has to reconcile

Depth vs. affordability

World-class specialists are scarce and expensive; no one project can justify retaining seven disciplines at depth on its own.

Consistency vs. autonomy

Countries need tailored support, but the programme needs a harmonized results framework so impact aggregates and compares.

Delivery vs. oversight

Senior technical staff are best used for quality assurance and no-objection; volume delivery is better done by supervised consultants and providers.

Action now vs. legacy

Money spent must also build lasting capacity and knowledge that outlives the project cycle — not just buy outputs.

The DSL-IP's answer is an explicit two-pool financial architecture, executed through One-FAO and anchored in a subregional hub. The rest of this note shows how it works — in flows and proportions rather than line items.

The Core DistinctionTwo pools, one flow of support

The architecture rests on a deliberate separation between who pays and what is funded. Two distinct resource pools feed a single, pooled stream of technical support that fans out to every country.

Pool 1 · Child Project LTO Fees

Every child project pays FAO a Lead Technical Officer fee. This pool covers the time of technical-division focal points — senior FAO staff who provide oversight, no-objection and quality assurance. It is spent sparingly: a few high-value days per task, per division. These days are marked “fee covered.”

Pool 2 · GCP Project Budget

The Global Coordination Project pools global resources to fund the delivery layer: international consultants, the Regional Coordinator, finance & admin support, travel, and regional Letters of Agreement with service providers. These are marked “GCP covered” — and they serve all countries at once.

Figure 1 · The pooling flow   Two pools converge into one shared stream of technical support, then fan out into the instruments that serve all eleven countries. Band widths are schematic — clay flows are fee-covered (oversight), green flows are GCP-covered (delivery).
Why split this way

Senior technical staff are the scarcest, most expensive resource — so the fee pool uses them sparingly and strategically, for the judgement-heavy tasks only they can sign off: no-objection on TORs, technical clearance of reports, selection panels. The GCP pool then funds the volume of delivery at a lower unit cost through consultants and providers — supervised by those same focal points. Quality at the top, capacity underneath.

The MultiplierOne fee day, many support days

The clearest way to see the architecture is task by task. For each workstream, the child-project fee buys a small slice of senior focal-point time — and the GCP matches it with a far larger block of pooled consultant time delivering to all countries. The ratio is the leverage.

The efficiency, doubly

The multiplier works on two axes at once. First, volume: a handful of senior days unlocks dozens of delivery days. Second, unit cost: focal-point days are senior-rate, used only where judgement is required, while pooled consultant days are procured at a lower rate for the bulk of delivery — and a single consultant's product (a tool, a training, a methodology) is then reused across every country, so the cost per country falls again. Pooling converts a senior-rate oversight budget into a programme-wide delivery engine.

Behind the ScenesOne-FAO — seven divisions, one officer

What looks, from a country's perspective, like a single Lead Technical Officer is in fact seven FAO technical divisions working as one. Each contributes its specialty behind the scenes; each appears on the workplan as a named focal point with a defined slice of days. The LTO integrates them into a single workplan and a single point of contact — so the country deals with one interlocutor, not seven procurement lines.

One integrated workplan, not seven parallel ones

The divisions do not operate in parallel silos. At inception they jointly develop one integrated regional technical-support and oversight plan — ensuring the programmatic approaches (FFS, seed banks, FFF, private-sector engagement) are closely interlinked rather than delivered as disconnected components. This is the financial expression of the integrated-landscape philosophy that runs through the whole DSL-IP.

Leveraging What ExistsThe Subregional Office as the hub

The architecture's final efficiency is physical. Rather than standing up a new coordination unit, the DSL-IP anchored its machinery at FAO's Subregional Office for Southern Africa (SFS) — an office that already exists, with staff, systems and standing relationships across the region. The Lead Technical Officer sits there. So does the Regional Exchange Mechanism's Regional Coordinator. Co-location is not an accident; it is the design.

Figure 4 · The SFS hub   One office hosts both the Lead Technical Officer and the REM Regional Coordinator — linking the eleven child projects and One-FAO divisions (left) to the regional bodies, existing programmes and knowledge hubs already present in the subregion (right).

Shared overheads

Office, systems, finance and admin already exist — the programme adds a coordinator and a finance/admin officer, not a whole new unit.

Standing relationships

SFS already works with SADC, the Great Green Wall South and national forestry institutions — the REM plugs into those ties rather than rebuilding them.

Engagement by proximity

LTO and Regional Coordinator in the same office means daily, informal coordination — the cheapest and fastest kind.

From Pooling to ScalePooling drives scalability and knowledge flow

Pooling is not only cheaper — it is what makes the programme scalable and self-harmonizing. Because the same pooled consultants, tools and service providers serve every country, a methodology developed once is deployed everywhere, and a lesson learned in one country flows to the rest through the very people who carry it.

01
Pool the resources

Country fees + GCP global resources combine into one technical-support budget, procured once for all.

02
Standardize the tools

Shared consultants build common methodologies — ILAM, the SLPF, harmonized M&E — against one results framework.

03
Deploy across countries

The same experts and providers roll the tools out in every country, adapting at the margins, not rebuilding.

04
Flow knowledge back

Lessons travel through the shared team and the REM — up to policy, out across borders, deep into practice.

A common results framework is the multiplier's hidden enabler

Pooling only scales if outputs are comparable. Because every child project reports against the same programmatic results framework and GEF-7 core and LDN indicators, the pooled team can compare, aggregate and transfer results across the portfolio. The financial design and the knowledge design are the same design: shared funding buys shared methods, which produce shared, scalable evidence.

Letters of Agreement · pooling the delivery partners too

The same logic extends to implementation. Rather than each country contracting its own providers, the GCP establishes regional Letters of Agreement for the core approaches — Farmer Field Schools, the Forest & Farm Facility, Community Seed Banks, and an on-demand service window. Aligned to the countries' harmonized technical demands, these pooled LoAs spread fixed costs, guarantee consistent quality, and let a specialist partner (IIRR, IIED, CTDT and others) serve the whole region at once.

Across the Project CycleHow the fee workplan unfolds

The architecture is operationalized through the LTO cluster fee workplan — a single, integrated plan spanning the full 2021–2026 cycle. Focal-point and consultant days are allocated phase by phase, front-loaded at inception when tools are set up, then sustained through implementation as oversight.

Inception
Set up the system. Draft and clear TORs and LoAs; develop the integrated regional support plan; prepare and conduct tool training; participate in selection panels and the inception workshop. Heavy on shared consultant days; sparing on senior fee days.
Country docking
Plug each country in. The Regional Coordinator (50% allocated to docking) aligns GCP support with each project's work and budget plan; countries join the MEL and KCO working groups.
Implementation
Sustain quality. On-demand annual backstopping missions; review of progress reports and technical products; no-objection on annual work plans and budgets; six-monthly PPRs and annual PIRs — senior fee days used precisely where sign-off is required.
Review & close
Safeguard the legacy. Comment on mid-term and final evaluations; support adjustment plans; embed sustainability of outputs beyond the project — ensuring the capacity built outlasts the financing.
Two travel budgets, one logic

Even travel is split along the two-pool line: staff on-demand travel for focal-point missions sits in the fee budget, while consultant travel sits in the GCP project budget — with virtual-first delivery (webinars, remote training) keeping both lean. The principle is constant: the fee pays for judgement, the GCP pays for reach.

The PayoffWhy this architecture works

The financial design is not an accounting convenience — it is the mechanism that lets a modest, distributed budget deliver world-class, harmonized support to eleven countries and build lasting capacity.

01 Depth without the price tag

Seven disciplines at depth — affordable only because senior time is pooled and used sparingly, and delivery is shared.

02 Built-in harmonization

Shared funding buys shared methods, so results are comparable and the portfolio aggregates against one framework.

03 Knowledge that travels

The same pooled team carries lessons across borders — scaling up, out and deep through the REM.

04 Low marginal cost to add a country

Because the engine is shared, onboarding a new champion country costs a docking process, not a new support unit.

05 Leverages existing assets

The SFS subregional office, FAO's 80 years of expertise, and ongoing regional programmes are reused, not rebuilt.

06 Sustainability by design

The model channels spending into capacity and knowledge that persist beyond the project's financing window.

The bottom line

One-FAO behind the scenes; two pools of resources; one Lead Technical Officer; one subregional hub. By pooling country fees with global coordination resources and anchoring them in existing infrastructure, FAO converts eleven constrained national budgets into a single, deep, scalable engine for dryland transformation — delivering global scalability and knowledge flow to countries that share one programmatic results framework.

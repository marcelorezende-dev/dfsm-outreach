Why Türkiye makes the case

Türkiye has emerged as a global leader in dryland forestry — committing to restore 2.3 million hectares by 2030 under the Bonn Challenge, lifting forest cover from 26% to 30% of its land despite ~65% being arid or semi-arid, and training forestry practitioners from some 25 countries through its International Forestry Training Centre. It also serves on the FAO COFO Working Group on Dryland Forests. The host of COP31 is living proof that dryland forests can be restored at scale.

Why dryland forests — why now

The forests climate finance forgot

Dryland forests, woodlands and the billions of trees outside forests are a nexus solution — delivering climate resilience, biodiversity and livelihoods at once. Yet they are systematically under-recognized, under-financed and inadequately monitored across every global agenda.

The Policy Gap

No dedicated global mechanism coordinates dryland forest action across regions. They are weakly reflected in NDCs, NAPs, NBSAPs and LDN targets, with governance fragmented across sectors.

The Finance Gap

Investment stays short-term and project-based. Private capital is scarce — held back by perceived risk, missing early-stage finance and the absence of investor pipelines for regenerative dryland enterprises.

The Knowledge Gap

Proven SLM/SFM solutions exist but are not exchanged across similar biomes. Data and monitoring gaps cause degradation and restoration needs to be systematically underestimated.

The TFFF parallel

If tropical forests have the TFFF,
drylands need the DFSM

The TFFF proved a principle the world had long resisted: that keeping forests standing is worth paying for, at scale, with the Global South in the lead. The DFSM carries that same logic into the drylands — adapted to ecosystems where the threat is degradation, not just deforestation.

An honest distinction. The two are not identical instruments. The TFFF is a single results-based payment fund; the DFSM is a broader, FAO-anchored support mechanism that combines coordination, technical assistance and finance mobilization — building investor pipelines and blended finance rather than a single per-hectare payment. What they share is the breakthrough idea: a dedicated global mechanism, country-driven and built for a whole biome that the world had been financing in fragments.

What the DFSM is

A dedicated mechanism,
not another project

The DFSM is the direct operational response to the Call to Action on Dryland Forests. It works through a simple, demand-driven cycle — and transforms dryland landscapes from extractive to regenerative management through three mutually reinforcing pillars.

01 · EVIDENCE

Collect

Close critical data gaps on the extent, status and trends of dryland forests through comprehensive assessments — strengthening the evidence base to guide decisions and target investment.

02 · IMPLEMENTATION

Act

Put proven SLM/SFM practices to work at landscape scale — translating evidence into action and using South–South exchange to accelerate uptake across biomes and regions.

03 · LEARNING

Track

Monitor progress toward sustainable management and restoration with harmonized indicators and participatory systems — assessing what works, at what scale, and feeding it back into global policy.

Built on a proven foundation

The DFSM doesn't start from scratch. It leverages the GEF-7 Dryland Sustainable Landscapes Impact Program (DSL-IP), FAO's first Global Dryland Forest Assessment, and 80 years of forestry experience — under a Multi-Donor Trust Fund structure linking national action to global support.

What we are calling for

A coordinated global response

The Call to Action on Dryland Forests urges governments, donors, international organizations and private partners to act collectively and decisively. Here is what each is asked to do.

Governments
  • Endorse the Call to Action as a shared framework for dryland forests.
  • Integrate dryland forests into NDCs, NAPs, NBSAPs and LDN targets.
  • Prioritize them in national planning and budgets, with cross-sectoral coordination.
  • Engage the DFSM to advance country-led priorities and collective learning.
Donors & IFIs
  • Provide predictable, multi-year, programmatic financing — beyond short-term projects.
  • Align support with country-led priorities and the DFSM's objectives.
  • Invest in data, monitoring and reporting that unlock results-based finance.
  • De-risk private capital to build investor pipelines for dryland enterprises.
UN & Conventions
  • Strengthen coherence and joint programming across UN agencies.
  • CBD: integrate dryland forests into restoration (Target 2) and sustainable use (Target 10).
  • UNFCCC: recognize, finance and monitor dryland forests in climate action.
  • UNCCD: recognize them as a priority ecosystem within LDN implementation.

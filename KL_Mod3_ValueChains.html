Making restoration pay

Rapid sustainable value-chain assessment — finding the economic entry points that make sustainable land and forest management financially attractive to the people who must deliver it.

Biophysical assessment tells you where land is degrading. Value-chain assessment answers the complementary question: what economic activity can be strengthened or introduced so that conservation becomes worth a land user's while? It is the bridge from a degradation map to an investable plan.

It works at two levels at once

DiagnosticCharacterize the existing local economy — production systems, market channels, agents, formal and informal trade — to see what people already produce and where the system fails them.
StrategicIdentify the value chains with the greatest potential to raise income, improve food security, and incentivize SLM/SFM adoption at scale.

The harmonized method

A single replicable framework consolidates fifteen country assessments into four moves: a Baseline & Feasibility approach; multi-criteria prioritization scoring to rank candidate chains; a degradation–value-chain nexus analysis that classifies each chain as degradation-driving or restoration-aligned; and a gender-disaggregated actor mapping that makes women's roles visible across the chain.

Why it matters

It targets the SLM incentive

By classifying chains against the degradation nexus, it steers investment toward honey, indigenous fruits, NTFPs and other restoration-aligned chains — and away from degradation drivers — so income growth and land recovery pull in the same direction.

It feeds the rest of the system

Findings flow directly into the Sustainable Landscape Production Framework and the integrated capacity-development plan, and are cross-validated against household resilience data and the Simplified LADA field assessment.

2 · Description of the Technology

2.1Short description
Good-practice harvesting of native Caatinga products – carnaúba wax/fibre, licuri nut/oil, caroá fibre and umbu fruit – guided by a simple collection plan that leaves enough for the plant to regenerate and for wildlife, with clean handling and a path to organic recognition (MAPA, 2015).
2.2Detailed description

The Technology turns the living Caatinga into income, making a standing forest worth more than a cleared one. Collectors work from a collection plan: mapping the productive stands, deciding what, when and how much to harvest, and always leaving a share of fruit, seed and leaves for the plant to recover and for the fauna that depend on the same resource – most pointedly the licuri palm and the endangered Lear's macaw (Leal et al., 2003; MAPA, 2015).

Harvest follows species good practice from the MAPA guides: never fell the plant; cut or gather selectively, by hand, in season, with clean tools and hygiene. Carnaúba leaves are cut and dried to extract the cerose powder (wax); licuri nuts are gathered for food, oil and craft; caroá leaves yield fibre; umbu is collected for pulp. The raw product is cleaned, dried and processed (beneficiamento) to a marketable quality (MAPA, 2015; FAO, 2026).

Collectors organise in associations/cooperatives, keep collection records, and work towards organic recognition (Projetos Extrativistas Sustentáveis Orgânicos), which adds value and opens markets for a sociobiodiversity product. Land users value a standing-forest income that complements farming and grazing; constraints are price volatility, the demands of certification, and leaving genuinely enough for regeneration and wildlife (Brasil, 2003; MAPA, 2015).

4 · Technical specifications, activities and costs

4.1Technical specifications
  • Collection plan per stand: what / when / how much; defined harvest season per species
  • No felling; selective hand harvest; clean tools and hygiene (organic)
  • Retain a share of fruit/seed for regeneration and for fauna (e.g. licuri for Lear's macaw)
  • Processing (beneficiamento): drying, cleaning, wax/oil/fibre extraction to quality
  • Records kept for organic certification (OCS / participatory guarantee system; MAPA, 2015)
4.2Calculation basis
Basis
Per collector / per unit of product (kg wax, kg nut, kg fibre) · BRL

Reduced incentive to clear native Caatinga; conservation of keystone palms and their dependent fauna across the landscape; maintained habitat connectivity.
6.4 – 6.6Cost-benefit, adoption, adaptation
Cost–benefit
Positive where market and certification are in place; sensitive to price
Adoption
1–10% with good practice/certification; traditional extractivism itself widespread (WOCAT, 2019)
Adaptation
Yes – organic certification, value-adding and cooperative processing
6.7 – 6.8Strengths and weaknesses

Strengths

  • Makes the standing forest pay – direct conservation incentive
  • Sustains keystone species and iconic fauna
  • Low inputs; income for women and traditional communities

Weaknesses → remedy

  • Price volatility → cooperatives, value-adding, diversified products
  • Certification demands → participatory guarantee systems, support
  • Risk of over-harvest → collection plans; leave shares for fauna

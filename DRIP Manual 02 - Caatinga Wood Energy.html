Sustainable wood energy from the Caatinga

Firewood and charcoal on a managed cutting cycle – not by clearing the forest

A field guide for community associations and producers: how to supply legal fuelwood and charcoal through a Sustainable Forest Management Plan that lets the Caatinga grow back.

iIn this manual

By the end you will be able to:

  • Lay out a forest area as annual cutting blocks on a rotation.
  • Cut each block so it regrows – keeping seed trees and the water lines.
  • Legalise and sell your firewood or charcoal through a management plan.
  • Raise the yield from each tonne of wood with better charcoal practice.

1Why it matters

The Northeast runs heavily on Caatinga firewood and charcoal (MMA, 2008). The problem is not that the forest is cut, but how: clear-cutting strips the land for good, while planned management harvests on a cycle so each area regrows before it is cut again. Done legally, it turns a driver of degradation into a lasting, documented source of rural income (MMA, 2011).

2Before you start

This practice fits your situation if:

  • You have a forest area with regeneration capacity (Caatinga that sprouts back after cutting).
  • You hold tenure or CAR, with the legal reserve defined.
  • You can work as an association or community to carry a Sustainable Forest Management Plan (PMFS).
  • You have, or can reach, a legal market for documented firewood or charcoal.

3The practice, step by step

  1. Inventory and map the stand. Estimate the standing wood (st/ha) and mark the area going to productive management versus reserve and riparian buffers.
  2. Divide into annual cutting blocks. Split the production area into roughly equal blocks for an approximately 15-year cutting cycle – one block cut per year, then left to regrow (MMA, 2011).
  3. Cut this year's block in the dry season. Coppice/selective cut to near ground level so stumps resprout; keep scattered seed trees and never touch the riparian strips (MMA, 2011; Araújo Filho, 2013).
  4. Let the cut blocks regenerate. Protect regrowing blocks from fire and heavy grazing so they rebuild before their turn comes round again (MMA, 2011).
  5. Process efficiently. Dry the wood; for charcoal use improved kilns to get more product per tonne and less smoke (MMA, 2011).
  6. Sell it legal. Move the product with the documentation from your management plan, through the association.

4Do this – avoid that

Do

  • Harvest one block a year on a fixed cycle
  • Leave seed trees and all riparian vegetation
  • Operate under a legal management plan (PMFS)
  • Use improved kilns; document the product

Avoid

  • Clear-cutting whole areas at once
  • Cutting riparian zones or the legal reserve
  • Burning the cleared area to "finish" it
  • Selling undocumented, illegal wood or charcoal
The golden rules

1. Cut on a cycle – never re-cut a block before it has regrown (around 15 years).   2. Keep the renewers – leave seed trees and all riparian vegetation.   3. Stay within the plan – never exceed the volume and area your management plan authorises (MMA, 2011).

5Field checklist

  • Stand inventoried; production area, reserve and buffers mapped
  • Area divided into annual blocks for the cutting cycle
  • Management plan (PMFS) approved; tenure / CAR in order
  • This year's block cut in the dry season – seed trees kept
  • Riparian zones and legal reserve untouched; no fire
  • Regenerating blocks protected from fire and overgrazing
  • Product documented and sold through the association

6Adapt it with your community

How many blocks will your cutting cycle need, and who manages each year's harvest? How will the association share the work, the documentation and the income – and protect the regrowing blocks?

7How you'll know it's working

Look forGood sign
Regrowth on cut blocksVigorous resprouting; stand height rebuilding on schedule
LegalityEvery load backed by management-plan documents

Sisteminha-IFES Alignment with SLPF Guiding Principles

Sustainable Landscape Production Framework (SLPF)

The SLPF is the methodological backbone of [1] DSL-IP's integrated landscape approach. It breaks down traditional silos between agriculture and forestry through three pillars: Community Seed Banks (CSB) Forest & Farm Facility (FFF) Farmer Field Schools (FFS)

Sisteminha-IFES embodies SLPF's three key guiding principles: [1]

The Three SLPF Guiding Principles & Sisteminha-IFES Alignment

SLPF PRINCIPLE #1

Peer Learning

SLPF Approach: Farmer Field Schools (FFS) create platforms for peer-to-peer knowledge exchange, hands-on training, and farmer-to-farmer learning.

↓
Sisteminha-IFES Implementation:
  • Monthly Farmer Field Schools in Timvane & Umphawi Toto
  • Demonstration farms showcase all system components
  • Lead farmers trained to mentor 5-10 peers
  • Seasonal workshops on component management
  • Community seed groups share knowledge

Income Generation & Livelihoods

SLPF Approach: The Forest & Farm Facility (FFF) develops green value chains and integrates farmers into inclusive, sustainable markets — turning production into reliable income and stronger livelihoods.

SLPF's Innovative Scalability Approach: FFPOs as Levers of Change

Forest & Farm Producer Organizations (FFPOs) = Levers of Change

SLPF identifies Forest & Farm Producer Organizations (FFPOs) [1] [20] as the critical institutional lever for transforming landscapes at scale. Rather than creating parallel project structures, SLPF strengthens existing community institutions (farmer groups, cooperatives) to become the primary delivery mechanism for SLM interventions.

Key Innovation: FFPOs enable landscape-level change because they:

  • ✓ Provide organizational structure for collective action
  • ✓ Ensure benefit distribution is equitable and inclusive
  • ✓ Create accountability to community members
  • ✓ Persist beyond project timelines (institutional sustainability)
  • ✓ Enable geographic scaling (lead to expansion in surrounding areas)
  • ✓ Facilitate farmer-to-farmer knowledge transfer
SLPF's Systems Thinking: FFPOs as Landscape Actors

SLPF recognizes that achieving Land Degradation Neutrality at landscape scale requires transforming the institutional landscape, not just the biophysical landscape. This means:

  • Identify existing FFPOs in target communities
  • Strengthen their capacity in business development, market linkage, governance
  • Empower them as the primary agents delivering SLPF interventions
  • Embed sustainability by building their independence from external support
  • Enable scaling by positioning FFPOs as multipliers reaching beyond pilot sites

Result: FFPOs become embedded institutions that continue delivering SLM, seed banking, value chain services, and farmer training long after project closure.

What is Sisteminha?

WHAT IS SISTEMINHA?

Sisteminha = "little system" in Portuguese [6]

A HOLISTIC INTEGRATED FOOD PRODUCTION SYSTEM [6] combining:

  • ✓ Aquaculture (fish in tank)
  • ✓ Vegetable gardens
  • ✓ Poultry production
  • ✓ Livestock (rabbits, goats)
  • ✓ Composting & soil improvement
  • ✓ Agroforestry (tree integration)

All components work TOGETHER in a CLOSED-LOOP nutrient cycle.

KEY SLM TERM: Closed-Loop Nutrient Cycling

Nutrients move in cycles within the farm: Fish waste → plant nutrients → animal food → animal manure → soil improvement → plant nutrients (repeat).

Minimal external inputs; the system sustains itself. Opposite of linear systems where nutrients are removed and lost.

How Sisteminha Works - The Cycle

  1. FISH TANK (5,000L) - Fish eat feed → produce waste in water
  2. GARDEN IRRIGATION - Fish tank water flows to vegetables (no fertilizer needed)
  3. VEGETABLE GROWTH - Plants get nutrients and water
  4. ANIMAL PRODUCTION - Animals eat vegetables → produce manure
  5. COMPOSTING - Animal manure + plant waste → soil amendment
  6. SOIL IMPROVEMENT - Organic matter increases → water retention improves
  7. → CYCLE REPEATS - System sustains itself year after year
KEY SLM TERM: Integrated Food Production System

A farm system where multiple food production components (crops, fish, animals, trees) are deliberately linked so nutrients and water cycle through the system multiple times.

Benefits: Increases productivity, reduces waste, improves resilience. Example: If maize fails, fish + vegetables + eggs still provide food.

What is IFES?

WHAT IS IFES?

IFES = Integrated Food and Energy Systems [13]

In Malawi, IFES specifically means:

  • ✓ PIGEON PEA intercropped with MAIZE [14]
  • ✓ Pigeon pea grain = food protein + market product
  • ✓ Pigeon pea stems = fuel material (energy security)
  • ✓ Pigeon pea leaves = animal fodder
  • ✓ Nitrogen fixation = soil fertility improvement

This is ALREADY PROVEN in Timvane & Umphawi Toto! [2]

KEY SLM TERM: Pigeon Pea & Nitrogen Fixation

Pigeon pea is a drought-tolerant legume that FIXES atmospheric nitrogen through bacteria in root nodules.

Benefits:

  • ✓ Adds FREE nitrogen to soil (no chemical fertilizer needed)
  • ✓ Survives dry periods when maize fails
  • ✓ Provides protein-rich grain for human food
  • ✓ Stems used as fuel material (energy security)
  • ✓ Strong export market (India, UK, emerging USA/Europe)

This is SUSTAINABLE LAND MANAGEMENT because farmers reduce fertilizer costs AND improve soil health simultaneously.

Production Timeline (Malawi)

November-March: Rainy Season ▼
  • Pigeon pea + maize planted together
  • Both grow simultaneously without competition
  • Pigeon pea nitrogen-fixes → benefits maize + soil
April-May: Early Dry Season ▼
  • Maize harvested March-April
  • Pigeon pea continues growing until June
  • Harvest grain + collect leaves (fodder)
June-October: Peak Dry Season ▼
  • Pigeon pea stems dry out
  • Harvest stems for fuel material (cooking/heating)
  • Dry grain stored for consumption + market sale
KEY SLM TERM: Intercropping

Growing two crops together (pigeon pea + maize) in the same space without competition.

Benefits:

  • ✓ Different root depths (no water competition)
  • ✓ Nitrogen-fixing legume benefits cereal crop
  • ✓ More total food/income per unit land
  • ✓ Risk diversification (if one crop fails, other may succeed)

WHY RAINWATER HARVESTING IS EXPENSIVE (But Worth It!)

THE PROBLEM: Malawi receives 600-1,200mm rain, but 90% falls Nov-Mar [15]. The other 6 months (May-Oct) = ZERO RAIN = DRY SEASON = HUNGER SEASON

WITHOUT rainwater storage:

  • ✗ System only works Nov-Apr (4 months)
  • ✗ Food gap May-October (6 months)
  • ✗ No income during dry season

WITH rainwater storage (30-50m³):

  • ✓ Capture wet-season surplus
  • ✓ Use stored water dry season
  • ✓ Fish production May-Oct
  • ✓ Vegetables May-Oct
  • ✓ Income 12 months/year
  • ✓ Food security extended 6 months

The $660-1,050 investment IN WATER INFRASTRUCTURE SOLVES THE ENTIRE DRY SEASON PROBLEM.

System Components

Component 1: Pigeon Pea-Maize Intercrop (60-80 m²)

PIGEON PEA-MAIZE INTERCROP

Planting: November (onset of rains)
Maize rows: 75cm spacing
Pigeon pea: Intercalated between maize rows

Production:

  • Maize: 800-1,000 kg/season
  • Pigeon pea grain: 200-300 kg/season
  • Pigeon pea stems: 400-500 kg (dried, for fuel)
  • Fodder from pigeon pea leaves: Continuous

Key benefit: Pigeon pea nitrogen-fixes → reduces maize fertilizer need by 20-30%

Component 2: Fish Tank (5,000L)

FISH TANK FOR WATER & PROTEIN

Tank size: 5,000 liters (2m radius, 0.8m deep) [6]
Fish type: Nile tilapia (hardy, fast-growing)
Stocking: 75 fingerlings per cycle [6]

Production: 15-20 kg/year (2 cycles in rainy season) [6]

Daily management:

  • Feed: 1% of fish body weight
  • Buffer solution: pH maintenance (lime + gesso)
  • Sediment removal: Weekly
  • Harvest: Every 150 days

Integration: Tank water flows to vegetable garden (gravity-fed) = free irrigation + nutrients

Component 3: Vegetable Garden (40-50 m²)

YEAR-ROUND VEGETABLES

Rainy season (Nov-Apr): Diverse vegetables

  • Tomato, cucumber, beans, peppers, squash

Dry season (May-Oct): Drought-tolerant only

  • Amaranth (extremely drought-tolerant)
  • Moringa (leaves 12 months/year)
  • Hand-watered from stored rainwater

Production: 600-800 kg vegetables/year
(100-150 kg/month rainy season; 50-100 kg/month dry season)

Water: Fish tank drip irrigation + stored rainwater
Nutrients: Fish tank water + compost application

Component 4: Livestock

INTEGRATED ANIMAL PRODUCTION

Poultry: 20 hens → 7,300 eggs/year → $100-150 income
Rabbits: 20-30 animals → 40-60 kg meat/year → income
Goats: 3-5 animals → 15-25 kg meat/year + milk → income

Fodder sources:

  • Pigeon pea leaves (rainy season)
  • Dried tree leaves (dry season - Faidherbia, Sesbania)
  • Vegetable garden surplus

Manure production: Daily collection: 15-20 kg/week → goes to compost bin → returns to soil as fertilizer

Component 5: Composting

ENHANCED COMPOSTING SYSTEM

3-CHAMBER COMPOST BIN:

  • Chamber 1 (Fresh - 0-4 weeks): Active decomposition
  • Chamber 2 (Maturing - 4-12 weeks): Curing
  • Chamber 3 (Finished - 12+ weeks): Ready to use

Input sources:

  • Animal manure: 15-20 kg/week
  • Maize stalks + pigeon pea leaves (seasonal)
  • Household vegetable waste: 5-10 kg/week
  • Tree leaves from nitrogen-fixers

Output: 200-300 kg finished compost/year

Application:

  • Pigeon pea field: 5-10cm layer before planting
  • Vegetable garden: Mixed into beds

Component 6: Rainwater Harvesting

RAINWATER HARVESTING (KEY FOR DRY SEASON)

Catchment: Household roof gutters + structure roofs
Annual runoff: 50-80m³ (depending on roof area)

Storage infrastructure: 30-50m³ total

  • Primary tank: 15-20m³ (near fish tank, gravity-fed)
  • Secondary tank: 10-15m³ (household water)
  • Tertiary: 5m³ (emergency reserve)

Dry season usage plan:

  • April-May: Use 20-30% (early dry)
  • June-August: Use 50-70% (peak dry - essentials only)
  • September-October: Use final 20% (bridge to rain)

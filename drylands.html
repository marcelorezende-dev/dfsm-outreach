What are drylands?

Drylands are defined by water scarcity, not by heat: places where the land loses more water to evaporation and plant transpiration than the rain returns. This page sets out how those boundaries are drawn, why DFSA also embraces the “presumed drylands” — dry forests and woodlands that behave as drylands even where rainfall is higher — and what that fuller footprint means for the accelerator’s mandate.

01 · The definition

A boundary drawn by water

The world’s drylands are delineated by the aridity index (AI) — the ratio of average annual precipitation to potential evapotranspiration (P/PET). Where that ratio falls below 0.65, rainfall supplies less than about two-thirds of the water the atmosphere and vegetation could evaporate, and the land is classed as dryland. It is a measure of moisture deficit, which is why cool high plateaus and hot deserts alike can both be drylands.

The United Nations (UNEP, 1992) and the UNCCD adopted the AI < 0.65 threshold, and in 2007 the UN Environment Programme’s World Conservation Monitoring Centre (UNEP-WCMC) mapped the world’s drylands, subdividing them into four zones by how dry they are. That map — the one shown further down this page — remains the reference used by FAO’s global drylands assessments.

The four UNCCD/UNEP-WCMC zones run from true desert to the near-humid margin at 0.65. Presumed drylands sit just past that line — technically wetter, but dryland in character. DFSA works across the whole span.

02 · The aridity zones

Four zones of dryness

UNEP-WCMC (2007) divides drylands into four aridity zones. Together they cover roughly 6.1 billion hectares — about 41% of the Earth’s land surface. The two conventions that govern dryland policy draw their lines slightly differently across these zones.

Hyper-arid

AI < 0.05

True desert. Dominated by the Sahara and the Arabian Desert, with sparse, specialised life and almost no rain-fed production.

16% of dryland area

Arid

AI 0.05 – 0.20

Sparse vegetation, long dry seasons and thin, erosion-prone soils; the zone most exposed to desertification.

25% of dryland area

Semi-arid

AI 0.20 – 0.50

Grasslands, savannas and open woodlands where rainfall is low and highly seasonal — the heartland of pastoral and agro-pastoral livelihoods.

37% of dryland area

Dry sub-humid

AI 0.50 – 0.65

The least dry zone — Sudanian savanna, tree steppes and prairie. Most dryland forests occur here.

22% of dryland area
UNCCD — the desertification lens

The UN Convention to Combat Desertification defines drylands as arid, semi-arid and dry sub-humid lands, and excludes hyper-arid deserts — desertification is degradation of land that could otherwise be productive, so the driest deserts fall outside its scope.

CBD — the biodiversity lens

The Convention on Biological Diversity’s Programme of Work on Dry and Sub-humid Lands takes a wider view: it includes hyper-arid deserts, and in 2010 formally recognised additional areas with dryland features — the presumed drylands.

03 · The DFSA interpretation

Presumed drylands

Some landscapes are wetter than the 0.65 threshold, yet unmistakably dryland in behaviour: marked dry seasons, fire-shaped vegetation, dry forests and woodlands on poor soils. UNEP-WCMC (2007) mapped these as “presumed included” in the dryland definition, and the CBD adopted them. Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) calls them presumed drylands — areas with an aridity index at or above 0.65 but bearing the features of drylands.

They are far from marginal. Presumed drylands add 1,075 million hectares — taking global dryland cover from 41% to 48% of the Earth’s land surface. They hold about 322 million hectares of forest, and trees are present across roughly half of their area. Most lie in Africa (36%), Asia (27%) and South America (24%), concentrated in three great landscapes.

DFSA counts these as drylands. Excluding them would write off exactly the dry forests and agrosilvopastoral systems the accelerator exists to support — and the communities who depend on them. Recognising them widens the map of where dryland forest action is needed, and where investment can secure land degradation neutrality before these landscapes tip.

“Areas with an aridity index equal to or above 0.65 but having dryland characteristics — based on precipitation, duration of dry season, occurrence of fires, or vegetation types.”

— Definition of “presumed drylands”, UNEP-WCMC (2007); FAO Forestry Working Paper 30 (2022)

The Cerrado

174 M ha

The world’s most biodiverse savanna and Brazil’s “birthplace of waters” — dry forest, savanna and gallery forest under intense agricultural pressure.

Miombo–Mopane woodlands

Southern & Eastern Africa
266 M ha

A transboundary belt from Angola to Tanzania across eight countries — seasonally dry woodlands that underpin the livelihoods of tens of millions.

China · Central & East Asia
153 M ha

“The roof of the world” — alpine grasslands and rangelands, a vast carbon store and the source of Asia’s great rivers.

Together these three regions hold 592 million hectares — slightly over 55% of all presumed drylands worldwide.

05 · Why they matter

The role and value of drylands

Drylands are neither empty nor unproductive. They are home to a large share of humanity and hold a disproportionate share of the world’s trees, carbon and agricultural land — which is precisely why their degradation carries such heavy costs.

Livelihoods

Dryland forests make up nearly 28% of the world’s forests and sustain more than a billion people — through pastoralism, agroforestry, fuelwood, wild foods and non-timber forest products.

Forests & trees

When forest, other wooded land and trees outside forests are counted, trees are present across roughly half of the presumed drylands alone — a resource base for agrosilvopastoral systems.

Carbon & climate

Dryland soils and vegetation are a major carbon store — soils hold the second-largest carbon pool after the oceans, and their loss undermines climate mitigation.

Biodiversity & water

From the Cerrado’s 12,000 plant species to the plateau rivers that supply 1.5 billion people, drylands regulate water, host unique species and buffer against drought.

06 · A moving boundary

Trends: degradation and a shifting line

The dryland boundary is not fixed. Climate change is pushing it outward, while land degradation is hollowing out the drylands that already exist — making the presumed drylands both more valuable and more at risk.

~⅓of global land

Land is already degrading

Around one third of the global land area is affected by degradation to some degree, with drylands among the most exposed. The cost of inaction has been assessed at at least three times the cost of restoration in Asia and Africa.

→expanding

Aridity is spreading

Growing aridity is enlarging the world’s drylands and putting more land at the threshold. Presumed drylands are, in FAO’s words, areas “with dryland characteristics and at greater risk of becoming drylands in the near future” — a frontier worth securing now.

LDNthe target

A priority for land degradation neutrality

Because they are still relatively tree-rich and biodiverse, presumed drylands offer high returns on restoration and sustainable management — a priority for LDN commitments and the UN Decade on Ecosystem Restoration (2021–2030).

This is why the boundary interpretation matters operationally. Drawing the line at 0.65 alone would leave the Cerrado, the Miombo–Mopane woodlands and the Qinghai–Tibetan Plateau outside the dryland agenda. By working across the fuller, presumed-inclusive footprint, DFSA targets action where dryland forests are most extensive, most valuable and most at risk.

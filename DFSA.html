01

The case for an accelerator

Dryland forests cover nearly 28% of the world’s forests and sustain the livelihoods of more than a billion people — yet they receive a fraction of global conservation finance, and over 25% are already degraded. Agricultural expansion, overexploitation and climate change accelerate feedback loops of forest loss, land degradation and poverty, undermining national LDN targets, NDC commitments and global restoration goals. The DFSA exists to break that cycle — by closing three structural gaps at once.

Gap 01 · policy

The Policy Gap

No dedicated global mechanism coordinates dryland forest action across regions, leaving responses fragmented and siloed. Dryland forests and the livelihoods of the millions of people who depend on them remain insufficiently integrated into national and global policy frameworks, including NDCs, NAPs, NBSAPs and LDN targets.

Gap 02 · finance

The Finance Gap

Investment in dryland forests and regenerative enterprises remains far below what is needed. Private capital is constrained by perceived risk, limited early-stage and blended finance, and the absence of investable pipelines. As a result, opportunities to scale sustainable livelihoods, local value chains and landscape restoration remain limited.

Gap 03 · knowledge

The Knowledge Gap

Proven sustainable land and forest management (SLM/SFM) solutions exist but are not systematically exchanged across similar dryland biomes. Limited market access, weak incubation and scaling support, and poor knowledge exchange constrain scaling and reduce opportunities to strengthen livelihoods, resilience and sustainable economic development.

Why now — a critical window of opportunity

The political momentum is real: the UNFF20 decision on dryland forests, the Call to Action on Dryland Forests, FAO’s Global Drylands Assessment and an established COFO Working Group on Dryland Forests and Agrosilvopastoral Systems give the agenda institutional anchorage. The investment case is proven too — demonstrated agroforestry-business models de-risk and catalyse private-sector engagement at scale through structured investor pipelines.

And the accelerator does not start from scratch. It builds on the USD 104 million GEF-7 Drylands Sustainable Landscapes Impact Program operating in 11 countries across three regions, FAO’s first Global Dryland Forest Assessment and 80 years of forestry experience — under a Multi-Donor Trust Fund structure linking national action to global support. Coalition members gain immediate access to the Communities of Practice, Regional Exchange Mechanisms, knowledge hubs, forest genetic resources, capacity development, monitoring tools and green value chains catalogued on this platform.

The approach — Collect · Act · Track

A transformational, people-centred, integrative and multifunctional approach for long-term action — the direct operational response to the Call to Action on Dryland Forests.

Collect

Close critical data gaps on the extent, status and trends of dryland forests — evidence that improves decisions and targets investment.

Act

Put proven SLM/SFM practices to work at landscape scale, with South–South exchange and the REMs accelerating uptake across biomes.

Track

Monitor progress with harmonized indicators and participatory systems — what works, under what conditions, at what scale.

Institutionalise

Move from fragmented, project-based wins to dryland forest action embedded in national systems — anchored in the COFO Working Group.

02

Leveraging the DSL-IP knowledge structure

DFSA does not start from scratch. It plugs into machinery the Programme has already built.

The Global Coordination Project already runs a knowledge-management structure — the Regional Exchange Mechanism, the Communities of Practice, the MEL working group and a participatory dashboard. DFSA becomes the public face and durable memory of that structure: the place its outputs are gathered, connected and kept discoverable long after a given meeting or report.

The Platform Map is the connective tissue — every page of DFSA is a node, so adding a new knowledge product is simply lighting up where it belongs in the network.

Channelled through the REM

Regional exchange feeds country knowledge in; DFSA keeps it findable.

Curated by the Communities of Practice

LDN, SLM/SFM and Gender CoPs supply and quality-check the content.

Evidenced by MEL

The monitoring system and dashboard keep every claim sourced and current.

Mapped on the Platform Map

Each product takes its place as a node in the living network.

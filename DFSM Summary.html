Concept Note · At a GlanceWhat the DFSM is

Dryland forests cover nearly a third of the world's forests and sustain over a billion people — yet they receive a fraction of global conservation finance, and a quarter are already degraded. The DFSM is the direct operational response to the global Call for Action on Dryland Forests: a multi-country mechanism that integrates technical assistance, blended finance and knowledge management to transform dryland landscapes from extractive to regenerative management.

In one sentence

The DFSM consolidates the political momentum behind dryland forests into a structured, FAO-anchored platform — anchored in the Committee on Forestry (COFO) Working Group on Dryland Forests and Agrosilvopastoral Systems and building on the proven Dryland Sustainable Landscapes Impact Programme (DSL-IP) — that helps countries scale the avoidance, reduction and reversal of degradation through investment-ready capacity, catalytic finance and shared knowledge.

The three pillars

01  Technical Assistance (TA)

Strengthens producer organizations, supports integrated land-use planning, and develops bankable business models for investment readiness.

02  Blended Finance Vehicle (BFV)

Uses concessional loans, equity and guarantees to de-risk investments and attract private capital at scale.

03  Knowledge Management (KM)

Facilitates learning, harmonizes monitoring systems, and fosters policy integration for scalable, lasting impact.

Mutually reinforcing

TA builds the pipeline, BFV finances it, KM scales what works — each pillar de-risks the next, turning grants into recyclable investment.

The ChallengeThree structural gaps

Despite covering nearly 28% of the world's forests and sustaining the livelihoods of over a billion people, dryland forests receive a fraction of global conservation finance. More than a quarter are already degraded — driven by agricultural expansion, overexploitation, unsustainable extraction, and climate change acting as a threat multiplier that accelerates feedback loops of forest loss, land degradation and poverty. This undermines national LDN targets, Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) commitments and global restoration goals.

Policy Gap

No dedicated global mechanism coordinates dryland forest action across regions, resulting in fragmented, siloed responses. Dryland forests are poorly integrated into NDCs, NAPs, NBSAPs and LDN targets.

Finance Gap

Private-sector investment remains very limited due to perceived high risk, inadequate early-stage finance, and the absence of investor pipelines for regenerative dryland enterprises.

Knowledge Gap

Proven SLM/SFM solutions exist but are not systematically exchanged across similar biomes. Access to markets, incubation and scaling pathways remains weak; data gaps mask the true extent of degradation.

Converging pressures, irreversible risk

Dryland forests face converging pressures from agricultural expansion, overgrazing, unsustainable resource extraction, invasive species and climate change — risking irreversible feedback loops of forest loss and land degradation. The DFSM breaks these cycles by closing the policy, finance and knowledge gaps simultaneously, rather than one at a time.

A Critical WindowWhy now

Dryland forests have moved from the margins of global forest policy to recognized priority — the product of more than a decade of intergovernmental groundwork. That convergence of political momentum, proven implementation experience and a time-sensitive funding opportunity makes this a pivotal moment for coordinated, scaled action.

Political momentum

The UNFF20 decision on dryland forests, the Call to Action, the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) Global Drylands Assessment, and an established COFO Working Group give the agenda institutional anchorage.

Investment multiplier

Demonstrated agroforestry-business models de-risk and catalyse private-sector engagement at scale through structured investor pipelines.

A time-sensitive window

The GEF-9 cycle offers set-aside windows to transition from recognition to catalytic, investment-driven implementation — but the window is finite.

Responding to the Call for Action on Dryland Forests

The Call identifies five priority areas the DFSM operationalizes: cross-country cooperation and capacity development; integrated investment and programmatic approaches; mainstreaming dryland forests into national and global policy; knowledge, monitoring and reporting systems; and gender-responsive action and women's leadership.

PositioningDryland forests as nexus landscapes

Dryland forests are increasingly recognized as critical nexus landscapes at the intersection of the three Rio Conventions — central to achieving land degradation neutrality (UNCCD), biodiversity conservation (CBD) and climate resilience (UNFCCC) simultaneously. The same response hierarchy — avoid, reduce, reverse — delivers across all three at once.

The DSL-IP cross-sectoral lesson

Eco-region experience reveals a common chain of interrelated challenges: declining agricultural productivity (staple monocrops) increases direct pressure on dryland forests, making degradation hard to avoid; lack of integrated extension, seed resources and SLM/SFM models undermines the sustainable management needed to reduce it; and weak access to seed and markets erodes the value case to rehabilitate and reverse critically degraded lands. The DFSM addresses all three links at once.

The Transformational ApproachCollect · Act · Track

The DFSM applies a transformational, people-centered, integrative and multifunctional approach, structured as a continuous evidence-to-action-to-learning cycle.

01 · COLLECT
Build the evidence base

Address critical data gaps on the extent, status and trends of dryland forests through assessments integrating biophysical, socio-economic and behavioural dimensions — to inform targeted investment.

02 · ACT
Implement best practices

Identify national and regional priorities, facilitate adoption of proven SLM/SFM approaches, and use South-South exchange and REMs to accelerate uptake across biomes.

03 · TRACK
Monitor, learn, scale

Monitor progress with harmonized indicators and participatory systems; assess what works, under what conditions and at what scale; disseminate through policy platforms.

DFSM Support PillarsThree integrated pillars

The DFSM scales sustainable management of forested drylands through three integrated pillars — Technical Assistance, a Blended Finance Vehicle and Knowledge Management — each operationalized through a set of preliminary work packages.

Component 1 · Technical Assistance for scaling SLM/SFM and investment readiness

Investment pipelinesBankable modelsBusiness accelerationIntegrated land-use planning

Strengthens producer organizations, supports land-use planning, and develops bankable business models so dryland enterprises become investment-ready — the pipeline the finance vehicle then capitalizes.

  • 1.1  LDN baseline assessment and scoping to prioritize investment in line with global environmental benefits and national priorities.
  • 1.2  Global Environment Facility (GEF) project pipeline development and investment readiness.
  • 1.3  Capacity building for integrated SLM/SFM adoption; a Business Incubation & Acceleration Program (BIAP).
  • 1.4  Integrated land-use planning, focused on LDN and forest genetic resources.

Component 2 · Blended Finance Vehicle to de-risk and mobilize private capital

Finance platformMDB & FI partnershipsFinancial instrumentsRevolving finance

Uses concessional loans, equity and guarantees to de-risk investments and attract private capital at scale — channelling blended finance through local intermediaries to producer organizations and value-chain actors.

  • 2.1  Establish the DFSM blended-finance platform.
  • 2.2  Map and deploy GEF Non-Grant-Instrument-aligned financial instruments.
  • 2.3  Deploy finance solutions through local intermediaries to Forest and Farm Producer Organizations (FFPOs) and value-chain actors.
  • 2.4  Sustain financing through capital recycling, pipeline development and reinvestment models.

Component 3 · Knowledge Management for learning, monitoring and policy integration

LDN integrated monitoringKnowledge productsSouth-South cooperationPolicy mainstreaming

Facilitates learning, harmonizes monitoring systems and fosters policy integration — turning results into replicable models and embedding them in national policy and investment instruments.

  • 3.1  Support evidence-based decision-making and tracking aligned with national priorities.
  • 3.2  Enable cross-regional mapping, learning and replication of successful models.
  • 3.3  Scale successful DFSM models through integration in national policy and investment instruments.

From GEF to Scalable ImpactThe financing logic

The DFSM's financial architecture is designed to turn finite grant resources into a self-sustaining investment cycle — aggregating investments, reducing transaction costs and aligning stakeholder incentives so impact extends well beyond individual projects.

01
Blended finance structure

GEF grants enable system set-up and technical support; Non-Grant Instruments provide de-risking capital that mobilizes additional finance.

02
Country-oriented targets

Blended TA + NGI, with a limited country matching allocation, are channelled into FFPOs, SMEs and landscape-level SFM/SLM interventions.

03
Revenue & repayment

Improved outcomes generate revenue from value chains and carbon markets, supporting loan repayment and capital recycling.

04
System transformation

Aggregated investment and lower transaction costs align incentives and extend impact beyond the project cycle.

Country incentive · substantial support, limited pressure on STAR

Champion countries access a combination of global support and catalytic resources from GEF-9 incentive windows — the Land Degradation global set-aside (technical assistance, pipeline development, South-South collaboration) and the Non-Grant Instruments set-aside (a 3:1 incentive for concessional finance, guarantees and blended solutions) — with only limited use of national STAR allocations. The result: strong country ownership, in-country public-private partnerships, and financing sustained through capital recycling and reinvestment.

What the DFSM OffersBuilt on the DSL-IP foundation

The DFSM does not start from zero. Leveraging the USD 104 million GEF-7 DSL-IP architecture across three regions, the first Global Drylands Assessment, the work of FAO's Dryland Forests Team and the Global Fire Hub, coalition members gain immediate access to established tools, platforms and networks — under a Multi-Donor Trust Fund structure that links national action to global support.

Communities of Practice

Three programmatic CoPs — on LDN decision-making, good SLM/SFM practices, and gender-responsiveness — ready to onboard new member countries.

Regional Exchange Mechanisms

Operational REMs across eco-regions, providing platforms for South-South cooperation, cross-border coordination and demand-driven assistance.

Knowledge Hubs & Tools

A robust network of knowledge hubs and technical expertise, leveraging FAO's 80 years of forest and natural-resource management.

Forest Genetic Resources

Conservation and sustainable use of native dryland trees and plant genetic resources.

Capacity Development

Training for farmers, foresters and land-user groups to implement integrated dryland forest management.

Monitoring & Learning

Participatory monitoring and evaluation tailored to dryland landscapes — from remote sensing to household and behaviour change.

Green Value Chains

Proven business incubation, private-sector engagement and market linkages, with demonstration models across regenerative value chains.

Immediate access

New members inherit a decade of tools, networks and trust — collapsing the start-up time for dryland forest action.

Global Geographic ScopeTarget biomes & countries across four eco-regions

The DFSM applies the “one country, one theme, champion” logic of the DSL-IP across four dryland eco-regions — a global cohort that enables cross-regional learning, investment aggregation and the scaling of proven models. It is a five-year, Land-Degradation multi-focal program (with biodiversity and climate co-benefits), coordinated by FAO with partners including the World Bank, DBSA, International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN), IIED and IIRR.

Strategic Alignment & ImpactAligned to global goals, delivering measurable results

The DFSM is engineered to advance national commitments and global frameworks simultaneously — its expected results map directly onto the GEF-9 Land Degradation focal-area objectives and the converging agendas of the Rio Conventions.

FAO's Four Betters

Better Production

Sustainable, resilient dryland forest landscapes through integrated SLM/SFM.

Better Nutrition

Dryland forest products contribute to food and nutritional security for dependent communities.

Better Environment

Ecosystem services — carbon sequestration, biodiversity conservation, watershed protection.

Better Life

Stronger livelihoods, tenure security, gender equity and social inclusion for forest-dependent communities.

Expected results

Land degradation

Significant area under improved management; direct contribution to national LDN targets; reduced soil erosion and improved vegetation cover.

Climate change

Mitigation through avoided deforestation and enhanced carbon storage; strengthened adaptive capacity for vulnerable communities.

Biodiversity

Conservation of endemic flora and fauna; maintained ecosystem connectivity and wildlife corridors; enhanced ecosystem services.

Livelihoods

Community-based agroforestry businesses with increased income and resilience; women- and youth-led regenerative enterprises.

Anchored across the global architecture

The DFSM advances the three Rio Conventions — UNCCD Land Degradation Neutrality and drought resilience; the CBD Kunming–Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (Targets 2 and 10); and UNFCCC NDC implementation. It supports NDCs, NAPs and NBSAPs at national level; global restoration platforms including AFR100, the Bonn Challenge, Initiative 20x20, ECCA30 and the UN Decade on Ecosystem Restoration; and contributes to SDGs 1, 2, 5, 13 and 15. Governance is anchored in the inter-governmental COFO Working Group on Dryland Forests, responding to UNFF20 and the UN Strategic Plan for Forests 2030.

The institutional shift

The DFSM enables countries to move from fragmented, project-based successes toward the institutionalization of dryland forest action within national systems and international support architectures — consolidating momentum, aligning actors and resources, and delivering sustained, coordinated and transformative impact at scale.

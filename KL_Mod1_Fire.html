Tracking fire across the world's largest dry woodland

A 25-year, district-level read on burned area across the Miombo–Mopane ecoregion — using the same Trajectory and State logic as land productivity, applied to fire.

Fire shapes the drylands — ecosystems, carbon, biodiversity and livelihoods all turn on it. This assessment applies robust, temporally-sensitive indicators to MODIS burned-area data across the ecoregion, so fire is monitored as a measurable trend rather than reported as a one-off number.

How it works

What it found

Fire is declining — and the decline is deepening

Median slope −277 ha/yr; ~52% of districts in strong or moderate reduction; total annual burned area down ~14% (122.5→105.3 Mha). The sliding-window analysis shows the decline steepening over time. Tanzania, Zambia and the DRC drive the strongest reductions.

But corridors can buck the trend

Along the Lobito Corridor, burned area is rising (its share of ecoregion fire climbed from 20.3% to 28.4%), driven by the Zambian segment — exactly the kind of localized hot-spot that programmatic monitoring is meant to catch.

Why it matters

Run continuously through ILAM rather than as static reporting, fire becomes a practical ESG-compliance instrument — district-level evidence that meets IFC and EU Global Gateway standards, lets investors pinpoint high-risk areas, and directs restoration funds where they are needed most.

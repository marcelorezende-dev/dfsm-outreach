Native value chains of the Caatinga

Sustainable, organic extractivism – carnaúba, licuri, caroá and umbu

A field guide for collectors and associations: how to harvest the Caatinga's native products well, so a standing forest pays more than a cleared one – and earns organic recognition.

iIn this manual

By the end you will be able to:

  • Plan a harvest that leaves enough for the forest to renew and for its wildlife.
  • Harvest and process native products to a clean, marketable quality.
  • Organise collectively and work towards organic recognition.
  • Tell the story that lets buyers pay more for a standing-forest product.

1Why it matters

Umbu (FAO, 2026), carnaúba wax, licuri and caroá fibre turn the living Caatinga into income – making the standing forest worth more than the cleared one. Harvested with good practice and recognised as organic, these sociobiodiversity products reward conservation directly, and keep the genetic and cultural wealth of the dry forest in local hands (MAPA, 2015).

2Before you start

This practice fits your situation if:

  • You have productive native stands – carnaúba, licuri, caroá, umbuzeiro or similar.
  • You can keep simple records of where and how much you collect (needed for organic recognition).
  • You can work in an association or group to reach buyers and certification.
  • You are ready to leave a share for regeneration and for wildlife that depend on the same plants.

3The practice, step by step

  1. Map your collection area. Note the stands, the access routes and any rivers or landmarks; record who has the right to collect there.
  2. Make a simple collection plan. Decide what, when and how much to take – always leaving fruit, seed and leaves enough for the plant to recover and for the fauna.
  3. Harvest with good practice. Never fell the plant. Cut or gather selectively, by hand, with clean tools and good hygiene; respect the species' season (MAPA, 2015).
  4. Process to quality. Clean, dry and handle the product carefully (e.g. carnaúba leaf, licuri nut, caroá fibre) to reach a price-worthy standard (MAPA, 2015).
  5. Organise and certify. Join an association; keep your records and work towards organic recognition (participatory guarantee or social-control body) (Brasil, 2003; MAPA, 2015).
  6. Sell the story. Market the product with its sociobiodiversity origin – a standing-forest, community product.

4Do this – avoid that

Do

  • Leave fruit and seed for regeneration and for fauna
  • Harvest in season, by hand, with clean tools
  • Keep collection records; pursue organic status
  • Add value and organise collectively

Avoid

  • Felling palms or taking the whole crop
  • Over-harvesting a stand year after year
  • Leaving nothing for wildlife (e.g. licuri for Lear's macaw; Leal et al., 2003)
  • Any chemical contamination of an organic product
The golden rules

1. Leave enough behind – for the plant to regenerate and for the fauna that share it.   2. Keep it clean and recorded – quality and traceability are what earn organic status (Brasil, 2003; MAPA, 2015).   3. Organise – a group reaches markets, certification and a fair price that an individual cannot (MAPA, 2015).

5Field checklist

  • Collection area and stands mapped; collection rights clear
  • Collection plan set – what, when and how much
  • Share left for regeneration and for wildlife
  • Harvested in season, by hand, no felling
  • Product cleaned, dried and handled to quality
  • Collection records kept; organic recognition pursued
  • Group organised; buyer and price agreed

6Adapt it with your community

Which native products does your area offer, and what season suits each? How much must you leave for the forest and its wildlife – and how will your group share the work, records and income towards organic recognition?

7How you'll know it's working

Look forGood sign
Healthy standsPlants regenerating; young palms and seedlings coming up
WildlifeFauna still feeding on the same plants (e.g. macaws on licuri)
Quality and statusClean, traceable product; organic recognition achieved
LivelihoodBetter, fairer price; income from the standing forest
